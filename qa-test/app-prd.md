# Cognitize: Product Brief for AI Development Agent

---

## 1. Vision & Mission

### What This App Is

Cognitize is an AI-powered speech coaching application that trains users to speak confidently under real conversational pressure. It is not a passive recording tool. It is not a language learning app. It is an active, adversarial conversation partner that interrupts users mid-sentence, fires unexpected follow-up questions, pivots topics without warning, and simulates the unpredictable dynamics of live speaking situations — job interviews, investor pitches, hostile Q&A, classroom presentations, and high-stakes debates.

The core thesis: **you cannot prepare for pressure by practicing without pressure.** Every other tool on the market asks users to speak into a void and then judges them afterward. Cognitize puts you in the fire while you're still speaking.

### Who It Serves

Cognitize exists for anyone who must perform verbally under conditions that cannot be fully controlled: the professional going into a board presentation, the job seeker preparing for a behavioral interview, the student who freezes during class discussions, the entrepreneur rehearsing a pitch for skeptical investors, and the person who simply knows they go blank under pressure and wants to fix that.

Primary audience: iOS users aged 18–40 with a specific, near-term high-stakes speaking event motivating them to practice. Secondary audience: habitual self-improvers who incorporate speaking practice into regular professional development.

### Why This App Must Exist

Public speaking anxiety affects an estimated 75% of the population. The demand for speaking improvement tools is enormous and growing — remote work, video interviews, Zoom presentations, and AI-saturated job markets have raised the stakes for verbal communication. Yet the current app market fails this audience in three compounding ways:

**First**, the dominant tools are passive. Apps like Orai and Credible record your speech and analyze it afterward. This is the equivalent of learning to drive by watching yourself drive on video. It produces awareness, not reflexes. The moment a real interviewer cuts you off or a skeptical investor challenges your assumptions mid-sentence, no amount of playback practice prepares you. Users feel false confidence from passive tools and are blindsided in real situations.

**Second**, the feedback is either opaque or inaccurate. Users report that black-box scores shift unexplainably between sessions, that "confidence ratings" are never defined, and that AI-generated feedback sounds generic regardless of what they actually said. Trust in the feedback collapses, and without trustworthy feedback, the tool becomes useless regardless of its features.

**Third**, the monetization is hostile. Competitor reviews consistently surface aggressive paywall patterns: basic features locked behind subscriptions that are never communicated upfront, trials that auto-charge without clear reminders, and cancellation flows deliberately buried in settings. Users who might have become long-term customers become angry detractors. This is not just an ethical failure — it is a strategic failure that suppresses organic growth.

Cognitize fills all three gaps simultaneously: active pressure simulation, honest explainable feedback, and ethical monetization with a genuinely useful free tier.

### Core Market Insight

The gap is not in technology — Orai has AI, Credible has AI. The gap is in **design philosophy**. Every competitor was built around the assumption that users need to be measured. Cognitize is built around the assumption that users need to be challenged. Measurement is a byproduct of the challenge, not the product itself. This inversion is the entire competitive thesis.

## 2. User Personas & Their Problems

### Persona 1: Marcus — The Career Climber

**Who He Is**
Marcus is 28, a mid-level marketing manager at a mid-size technology company. He is technically competent and well-liked by his team, but he is being passed over for a director-level promotion because he stumbles in executive presentations. His manager has told him directly, in feedback, that he needs to be "more commanding in the room." He has the ideas. He falls apart when someone challenges them out loud.

**Context**
Marcus has a quarterly business review presentation coming up in six weeks where three VPs will be in the room. He has rehearsed the deck alone in his apartment. He knows the material cold. But the last time he presented, a VP interrupted him with a question he hadn't anticipated, and he lost his thread entirely and never recovered. That moment replays in his head.

**Frustrations with Existing Solutions**
Marcus downloaded Orai, recorded himself presenting, watched the playback, and received a score. The score told him he used too many filler words. He already knew that. It did not help him with the VP who interrupted him. He tried practicing in front of a mirror. He tried recording himself on his phone. None of it created the pressure of the actual moment. He does not need to know what he looks like when he presents. He needs to be interrupted and learn to recover.

**What Success Looks Like**
Marcus completes six weeks of pressure sessions in the "High-Stakes Presentation" scenario. By the end, when the AI fires a sharp challenge mid-sentence, he pauses, acknowledges the question, and answers directly without losing the thread of his original point. He walks into the quarterly business review having experienced that exact dynamic a hundred times. His score history shows measurable improvement in response coherence under interruption. He gets the promotion.

**Day-in-the-Life Scenario**
It is 7:15 AM. Marcus has 20 minutes before he needs to leave for work. He opens Cognitize, selects his saved "Executive Presentation" scenario set to Difficulty 4 (of 5). He starts speaking — opening remarks from his upcoming deck. Forty-five seconds in, the AI interrupts: *"Before you continue — what's the ROI evidence for the campaign you just described?"* Marcus hesitates. He recovers. He answers. The AI does not let him finish the answer cleanly — it pivots: *"That's a 2022 study. What's your current data?"* The session runs twelve minutes. Afterward, Marcus sees that his average recovery time after an interruption dropped from 4.2 seconds to 2.8 seconds over the past two weeks. That number matters to him.

### Persona 2: Priya — The Ambitious Job Seeker

**Who She Is**
Priya is 24, a recent MBA graduate, actively interviewing for roles at consulting firms and early-stage startups. She is analytically strong and prepares extensively for interviews — she knows frameworks, she knows case structures, she has written out answers to the 30 most common behavioral questions. Her problem is that under live interview conditions, she over-explains, buries her punchline, and loses the interviewer's attention before she gets to her point.

**Context**
Priya has a final-round interview at a consulting firm in two weeks. The format includes a behavioral interview with two partners who are known for interrupting candidates. She has practiced with friends, but her friends are too kind — they let her finish every sentence and don't ask the hard follow-up questions. She needs adversarial practice from someone who won't spare her feelings.

**Frustrations with Existing Solutions**
Priya used a speech coaching app during her undergraduate years. She found the feedback generic and the scenarios unrealistic. The app offered "Job Interview Practice" as a category, but the AI simply waited for her to finish each answer and then scored her on filler words. No real interviewer does that. She gave up on the app because the practice felt nothing like the actual experience. She has since been paying $150 an hour for a human speaking coach — valuable, but not available at 11 PM the night before an interview when anxiety peaks.

**What Success Looks Like**
Priya uses the "Consulting Interview" scenario at difficulty level 3, escalating to 4 over two weeks. The AI plays an impatient partner who cuts off verbose answers with *"Give me the headline first."* Priya learns to lead with her conclusion, then support it. Her feedback shows her average time-to-main-point dropped from 38 seconds to 11 seconds. She also uses the session history to identify that she uses the phrase "I think that..." as a confidence-undermining qualifier 14 times in a typical session. She stops. She gets the offer.

**Day-in-the-Life Scenario**
It is 11:30 PM. Priya's interview is tomorrow. She is too anxious to sleep. She opens Cognitize and starts a 15-minute "Behavioral Interview — Consulting" session. She begins answering *"Tell me about a time you led through ambiguity."* Twenty-three seconds in, the AI interrupts: *"What was your specific role? I'm not hearing that yet."* Priya restarts her answer leading with her role. The AI fires a follow-up before she finishes: *"What did the team do when you made that call?"* The session surfaces exactly the gaps a real interviewer would find. When Priya sits down across from the partner tomorrow, she has heard these exact interruption patterns before. The pressure is familiar. That familiarity is the product.

### Persona 3: Dev — The Student Finding His Voice

**Who He Is**
Dev is 20, a junior at a state university studying computer science. He is introverted, intellectually confident, and deeply uncomfortable with public speaking. He has a required public speaking course this semester and a capstone project presentation at the end of the year that will be seen by company recruiters. His anxiety is not performance anxiety in the theatrical sense — it is the specific fear of being questioned by someone who knows more than he does, or being asked something he cannot answer and going visibly blank.

**Context**
Dev does not have money to spend on premium apps. He needs a free tool that is actually useful, not one that teases usefulness behind a paywall. He is technically literate and will immediately identify dark patterns or misleading UX — and he will delete the app and leave a 1-star review.

**Frustrations with Existing Solutions**
Dev tried a free tier of a competing app and found that every meaningful feature was locked. The free tier let him record a 60-second speech and showed him one metric — a "clarity score" — with no explanation of how it was calculated and a prompt to upgrade to see the rest. He felt manipulated. He deleted the app in under five minutes.

**What Success Looks Like**
Dev uses the free tier of Cognitize consistently for eight weeks. He accesses the "Classroom Presentation" and "Technical Explanation" scenarios at difficulty 2, working up to difficulty 3. He can now hold his composure when someone asks a question he doesn't have a perfect answer to — he has a practiced response for uncertainty: acknowledge, contextualize, redirect. His capstone presentation earns specific positive feedback from a recruiter about his composure under questions. He decides Cognitize helped and, when he has income, he becomes a paying user.

**Day-in-the-Life Scenario**
Dev has 30 minutes between classes. He sits in the campus library, earbuds in, and runs a 10-minute "Technical Explanation" scenario at difficulty 2. He is explaining a concept from his project. The AI, playing a skeptical non-technical evaluator, asks *"Can you explain that without the jargon?"* Dev stumbles. He tries again. The AI follows: *"How does this compare to the existing solution? Why is yours better?"* These are the exact questions the recruiter will ask. Dev knows this. He runs the scenario again with slightly different framing. The second attempt is noticeably cleaner. He sees that in the session comparison view.

### Persona 4: Elena — The Entrepreneur Pitching for Her Company's Life

**Who She Is**
Elena is 34, the founder of an early-stage B2B SaaS startup. She has a warm intro to a Tier 1 VC firm in three weeks. She is a strong communicator in one-on-one settings but has never pitched a skeptical institutional investor. She knows investors interrupt, challenge assumptions, test founders on market size, and look for cracks in conviction. She has one shot.

**Context**
Elena has the pitch deck. She has practiced the narrative flow. What she needs is pressure. She needs someone to tell her the TAM is wrong, to challenge her churn assumptions, to ask *"Why hasn't this been done before?"* in the middle of her product slide. She cannot pay a coach $300/hour for two weeks straight. She needs on-demand adversarial pitch practice.

**What Success Looks Like**
Elena runs fifteen investor pitch sessions over three weeks, escalating from difficulty 3 to difficulty 5. The AI challenges her on every weak point in her pitch. She discovers, through the feedback, that she hedges her conviction language heavily under pressure — saying "we think we can" instead of "we will." She trains this out. She walks into the VC meeting having been challenged harder than any human coach would dare. She is unshakeable. The meeting goes to a second round.

**Day-in-the-Life Scenario**
It is Saturday morning. Elena has two hours. She runs a full 20-minute "Investor Pitch — Series A" scenario at difficulty 5. The AI is playing a skeptical VC partner. Two minutes into her market opportunity slide, the AI interrupts: *"Your TAM number — where does that come from? That feels inflated."* Elena pushes back confidently. The AI doesn't back down: *"But if you remove enterprise, which you said takes 18 months to close, what's your realistic addressable market in year one?"* Elena had never been pushed on that specific angle. She fumbles. She flags it. She prepares an answer. She runs the session again the next morning. This time she's ready. This is the product working exactly as designed.

## 3. Core User Experiences (Screen-by-Screen)

### Onboarding Flow

**Screen: Splash / App Load**
The app opens to the Cognitize identity mark against a clean, high-contrast background. This screen persists for no longer than 2 seconds. If the app has already been set up, it routes directly to the home screen. If it is a first launch, it routes to the onboarding flow. There is no interstitial prompt, no review request, no notification permission on this screen.

**Screen: Onboarding — What Cognitize Does (Slide 1 of 3)**
A single, bold statement of what the app does, accompanied by a brief animated visualization of the core experience: a user speaking, being interrupted, recovering. No feature list. No bullet points. One sentence. A "Continue" action at the bottom. Users can tap through all three slides without reading them — the onboarding is never gated.

**Screen: Onboarding — How It Works (Slide 2 of 3)**
Shows the three-step loop: speak, get interrupted, get feedback. Short. Visual. Does not explain what AI models are used. Does not list pricing. One "Continue" action.

**Screen: Onboarding — Free vs. Premium (Slide 3 of 3)**
A plain-language, honest summary of what is free forever and what is in the premium tier. No asterisks. No fine print. Specific: "3 scenario categories, unlimited sessions, full feedback — free, forever. Premium adds 6 additional scenario categories and advanced analytics for $X/month." A "Get Started Free" primary action and a "See Premium" secondary link that opens the pricing screen before any account creation. The user knows exactly what they are getting before they give any personal information.

**Screen: Account Creation**
Email and password fields. Optional "Sign in with Apple" as a prominent alternative. No phone number required. No birthdate required. No "Tell us about yourself" multi-step survey. One screen, three fields maximum. The privacy note is a single sentence with a link to the full privacy policy — not a wall of legalese. Account creation is required to save progress, but this screen makes clear why: *"Your progress is saved to your account so you can pick up where you left off."*

**Empty state after account creation**: User lands on home screen with a welcome message and a prominent single call-to-action: "Start Your First Session." No empty dashboard with greyed-out charts. No "complete your profile" prompts. Get them into a session immediately.

### Home Screen

**Purpose**
The home screen is a launching pad and a progress anchor. It should communicate two things at a glance: where the user is in their development, and what they should do next.

**Populated State**
The screen shows:
- A greeting with the user's first name
- A "Continue" or "Start Session" card — the single most prominent element, showing the last scenario used or a recommended next scenario if this is an early session
- A streak or recent activity indicator (days active in the last 7)
- A compact progress summary: one or two headline metrics from their most recent session (e.g., "Last session: Recovery time improved by 0.4s")
- A horizontal scroll of scenario categories (free tier categories fully accessible, premium categories visible but marked)

**Empty State (New User)**
No charts, no streaks, no metrics. Just the welcome message and one action: "Start Your First Session." The scenario selector is pre-populated with beginner-appropriate suggestions.

**Loading State**
A subtle skeleton layout — placeholder shapes where content will appear — that loads within 1 second on any modern device. Never a spinner blocking the full screen.

**Error State**
If data fails to load, the session start button remains fully functional. The app never blocks the user from starting a session due to a data-loading failure. An inline, non-alarming message indicates that some history may not be visible right now.

**Navigation**
Bottom navigation with four items: Home, Scenarios, Progress, Settings. Always visible. Never hidden behind a hamburger menu on primary navigation.

### Scenario Selection Screen

**Purpose**
Users browse and select the speaking context they want to practice. This screen is both a catalog and a progression map.

**Populated State**
Scenarios are organized by category: Job Interviews, Investor Pitches, Classroom Presentations, Difficult Conversations, Impromptu Speaking, Debate Rounds, and Technical Explanations. Each category shows a brief description of what pressure tactics the AI uses in that context. Each individual scenario card shows:
- Scenario name
- Recommended difficulty range
- Estimated session duration
- Whether it is in the free tier or premium (clearly labeled, never hidden)
- A brief "pressure preview" — one example of the type of interruption or challenge the AI will use

**Free vs. Premium State**
Premium scenarios are visible in the list but marked. Tapping a premium scenario shows a clear, non-pushy explanation of what premium includes and the price, with a single "Upgrade" action and an equally prominent "Not now" option. No countdown timers, no "limited offer" language, no manufactured urgency.

**Difficulty Selector**
Each scenario has a difficulty setting from 1 to 5. Difficulty 1 = the AI waits longer before interrupting and asks gentler follow-ups. Difficulty 5 = rapid-fire interruptions, aggressive pivots, minimal recovery time between challenges. The current recommended difficulty is highlighted based on the user's session history. Users can override this at any time — no forced progression.

**Empty State (New User)**
All beginner scenarios in the free tier are immediately available and highlighted. The screen does not feel sparse — it feels like a starting point, not a locked gate.

**Scenario Detail Screen**
Tapping any scenario opens a detail view that shows:
- Full description of the scenario context (e.g., "You are presenting to two consulting partners who are known for interrupting. Expect questions about your methodology, your assumptions, and your confidence in your data.")
- Example interruption types this scenario uses
- Recommended preparation tips (brief — not a wall of text)
- Difficulty selector
- Session length selector (10 / 15 / 20 minutes)
- "Start Session" — the primary action

### Active Session Screen

**Purpose**
This is the product. This screen is where the core value is delivered. It must communicate state clearly, avoid visual distraction, and make the user feel like they are in a real conversation — not using an app.

**Pre-Session State**
A brief, minimal setup screen: the scenario name, difficulty level, session length, and a single instruction — *"Speak when ready. The AI will respond."* A large, clear "Begin" button. No tutorial pop-ups, no onboarding overlays during the session setup after the first use.

**Active Speaking State**
The screen is deliberately minimal. The user sees:
- A live waveform visualization that responds to their voice — confirming the microphone is active and the app is listening
- A subtle session timer (not a countdown — this is not a test with a time limit, it is a session with a suggested length)
- The current scenario context as a single line of text at the top (e.g., "Consulting Interview — Difficulty 3")
- No transcription visible in real time — this would distract the user from speaking naturally

**Interruption State**
When the AI interrupts, the visual shifts clearly to indicate it is the AI's turn. The AI's voice plays through the speaker. The waveform transitions to show the AI speaking. The user's microphone is temporarily deprioritized (though not muted — they can jump in). The interruption text appears on screen as the AI speaks, so users with hearing accessibility needs can follow along.

**Post-Interruption State**
After the AI finishes its interruption, the screen returns to active speaking state with a subtle visual cue indicating it is the user's turn. No explicit prompt text like "Your turn" — the visual and audio cues handle this. The experience should feel like a conversation, not a turn-taking game.

**Session Pause**
A small, accessible pause button is always visible but not visually prominent. Tapping it pauses the session, stops the timer, and shows a simple overlay: "Session Paused — Resume or End Session." Ending a paused session still saves whatever progress occurred and generates partial feedback.

**Session End (Natural)**
When the session timer concludes, the AI delivers a closing line appropriate to the scenario context (e.g., *"We're out of time — I'll be in touch"* for an interview scenario), and the screen transitions smoothly to the feedback screen. No abrupt cutoffs, no system UI intrusions.

**Session End (User-Initiated)**
If the user ends the session early, a brief confirmation: "End session now? You'll still get feedback on what you've shared." One tap to confirm, one tap to return to the session.

**Error States During Session**
- Microphone permission denied: shown before session starts with a clear instruction to enable permission in Settings. Never surfaces mid-session.
- Network interruption mid-session: an unobtrusive banner appears ("Connection interrupted — continuing in local mode"). The session continues. Some features may degrade gracefully — the AI may rely on simpler response patterns — but the session is never terminated by a network failure.
- AI service unavailable: the AI falls back to a pre-loaded set of contextually appropriate interruptions and follow-up questions for the selected scenario. The user is not informed of this fallback mid-session — the experience is maintained. A post-session note may indicate that the session used simplified AI mode.

### Post-Session Feedback Screen

**Purpose**
Deliver honest, specific, explainable feedback that the user can act on. Not a grade. Not a leaderboard position. A coaching debrief.

**Loading State**
A progress indicator while feedback is being generated — estimated 5–10 seconds. A brief message: *"Analyzing your session..."* This is not a fake wait — analysis is genuinely happening. If analysis takes longer than 15 seconds, a message explains why: *"We're running a detailed analysis — this takes a moment."*

**Populated State — Summary Card**
The top of the screen shows a session summary:
- Session length and scenario
- A headline observation — one sentence about the most significant thing that happened in this session (not a score)
- Three to five specific metric scores with plain-language descriptions of what each measures

The metrics shown are:
- **Filler Word Rate**: Count of filler words (um, uh, like, you know, so) per minute of speaking time. Shown as a number with context: *"4.2 per minute — within the range of most professional speakers. Target below 3."*
- **Recovery Time**: Average time (in seconds) between an AI interruption and the user's coherent response. *"3.1 seconds average. Decreased from 4.4 seconds in your last session."*
- **Pacing Consistency**: Variance in words-per-minute across the session. Not just average pace, but how much it fluctuated under pressure. *"Your pace dropped significantly after the second interruption — a common response to pressure. This session shows improvement."*
- **Response Coherence**: A qualitative assessment of whether responses addressed the AI's question directly or deflected. Shown as a percentage and explained: *"72% of your responses directly addressed the question asked. Here's what that means and how it was measured..."*
- **Confidence Markers**: Frequency of hedging language (I think, maybe, sort of, I guess) and assertive language patterns. Not a judgment of personality — a coaching data point.

**Score Explanation Panel**
Every single metric has an expandable explanation that describes exactly how the score was calculated, what data was used, and what would change the score in the next session. No black box. If the user does not understand why they scored the way they did, that is a failure of the app, not a mystery to be preserved.

**Benchmarking**
Scores are benchmarked against the user's own history, not against a generic "professional speaker" standard. A new user with two sessions sees: *"Compared to your first session."* A user with twenty sessions sees trends across time. There is no messaging that implies the user is failing against an external standard — all progress is relative to their own baseline.

**Session Transcript (Expandable)**
The full transcript of the session — both user speech and AI interruptions — is available in a scrollable view below the metrics. Key moments are highlighted: moments of strong recovery, moments where a filler word spike occurred, moments where the AI interrupted and the user's response was notably coherent or incoherent. This is a learning document, not a surveillance record.

**AI Coaching Note**
One to three sentences of personalized, plain-language coaching generated from the session. This is the only part of the feedback that is generative AI prose. Example: *"You handled the first three interruptions well, but your pacing dropped sharply after the question about your data sources. This is common when you're asked about something you feel less certain about. Try your next session with the 'Handling Uncertainty' scenario to work on this specifically."*

This note is never generic. It references specific moments from the session. If the AI cannot generate a specific note (service unavailable), the app shows nothing rather than a generic filler note.

**Actions from Feedback Screen**
- "Run This Scenario Again" — immediately starts a new session with the same scenario and difficulty
- "Try Suggested Scenario" — navigates to the scenario suggested in the coaching note
- "Save to Progress" — always done automatically; this button is a confirmation, not a required action
- "Share Session Summary" — generates a clean, shareable image of the headline metrics (no audio, no transcript) for the user to share if they choose

## 4. Feature Specifications

### Feature 1: Pressure Simulation Engine

**What It Does**
The Pressure Simulation Engine is the AI conversation partner that runs every session. It listens to the user speak in real time, identifies appropriate moments to interrupt, generates a contextually relevant interruption or follow-up question, delivers it in a natural voice, and then returns control to the user. The engine's behavior is calibrated by scenario type and difficulty level.

At difficulty 1: the engine waits for natural pauses in the user's speech (silences of 3+ seconds) before interrupting. Its questions are clarifying rather than challenging: *"Can you give me an example of that?"*

At difficulty 3: the engine interrupts during sentences, not just at pauses. It asks follow-up questions that probe assumptions: *"Where does that data come from?"* It occasionally pivots to a new topic without warning.

At difficulty 5: the engine interrupts frequently, sometimes multiple times per minute. It challenges claims directly (*"I don't buy that assumption — defend it"*), pivots topics abruptly, asks compound questions, and introduces mild adversarial framing (*"Your competitor would say the opposite — why are they wrong?"*).

The engine never interrupts during the first 30 seconds of any session, regardless of difficulty. This warm-up window allows the user to establish their train of thought before pressure begins.

**Why It Matters**
This is the entire product thesis. Every other feature exists to support this one. Users who practice only in silence develop fluency in calm conditions. They do not develop the cognitive resilience to maintain coherent speech under interruption. The Pressure Simulation Engine creates the exact conditions users fear, in a safe environment, repeatedly, until those conditions become familiar rather than terrifying. Familiarity is the antidote to speaking anxiety.

This feature addresses Marcus's core problem (losing his thread when interrupted), Priya's core problem (being cut off before finishing an answer), and Elena's core problem (being challenged mid-pitch on assumptions she hasn't bulletproofed).

**Where AI Enhances the Experience**
The AI listens to the specific content of what the user is saying and generates interruptions that are relevant to that content. If the user mentions a specific number, the AI may challenge that number. If the user describes a strategy, the AI may ask about risks. If the user uses confident language, the AI may probe whether the confidence is supported. The interruptions are not drawn from a static script — they are generated in response to what was actually said.

The AI also tracks the conversational context across the session. A follow-up question references something said three minutes earlier. This creates the feeling of a real conversation with a real person who is paying attention — not a chatbot firing random prompts.

**Fallback Behavior When AI Is Unavailable**
If the real-time AI service is unreachable, the engine falls back to a pre-loaded library of scenario-appropriate interruptions curated for each scenario category. These are high-quality, contextually appropriate questions prepared in advance. The session continues without interruption. The user does not receive a notification that the AI is in fallback mode during the session — post-session feedback will note that the session used enhanced static mode.

The fallback library contains at minimum 50 unique interruptions per scenario category per difficulty level. This is enough content for approximately 10 full sessions before significant repetition occurs. If the same fallback question has appeared in the user's last 3 sessions, it is deprioritized.

**Edge Cases**

- *User speaks very quietly or in a noisy environment*: The app detects low audio input levels and shows a gentle, non-alarming prompt: *"Having trouble hearing you clearly — try moving to a quieter spot or adjusting your microphone."* The session can continue, but the feedback quality note will indicate potential transcription issues.
- *User goes silent for an extended period (30+ seconds)*: The AI does not immediately interrupt a long silence — silences can be intentional pauses for thought. After 45 seconds of silence, the AI delivers a soft prompt appropriate to the scenario: *"Take your time — what were you saying?"* This is a recovery cue, not a punishment.
- *User speaks in a language other than English*: Currently, the app is English-only. If the transcription system detects predominantly non-English speech, the app shows a post-session note: *"Cognitize currently works best in English. We're working on additional language support."* The session is not terminated.
- *User speaks extremely fast (above 220 words per minute)*: Transcription accuracy may degrade. The AI may interrupt less frequently to avoid interrupting in the middle of a word rather than a phrase. Feedback will note the high speaking pace as a data point.
- *Headphones vs. speaker mode*: The app strongly recommends headphone use to prevent AI audio bleed into the microphone. If no headphones are detected, an informational note (not a blocker) appears before the session starts.

---

### Feature 2: Honest AI Feedback with Transparent Scoring

**What It Does**
After every session, the app generates a structured feedback report covering five specific metrics: filler word rate, recovery time, pacing consistency, response coherence, and confidence marker frequency. Each metric is presented with a score, a plain-language explanation of how that score was calculated, a benchmark against the user's own historical average, and a specific, actionable observation about this session.

The feedback is generated from the complete session transcript and audio, analyzed after the session ends for maximum accuracy. This is distinct from the real-time transcription used to power the interruption engine — post-session analysis uses higher-accuracy processing.

Scores never shift unexplainably between sessions. If a score changes significantly, the feedback explains why. If the user asks why (via a feedback clarification flow), the app provides a specific explanation referencing session data.

**Why It Matters**
The reason users abandon speech coaching apps is not usually that they stop wanting to improve. It is that they stop trusting the feedback. A score that cannot be explained is a score that cannot be acted on. Cognitize's feedback must earn trust, not demand it. Every score is a claim about the user's performance. That claim must be defensible with specific data from their specific session.

This feature addresses the core trust failure of every competitor. It directly answers Dev's frustration with unexplained "clarity scores" and Priya's frustration with feedback that feels generic regardless of what she said.

**Where AI Enhances the Experience**
The AI-generated coaching note at the end of the feedback is the one place where generative prose appears in the feedback section. This note identifies patterns that the metric scores alone would not surface — for example, noting that a user's filler word rate increases specifically when challenged on data, but not in other parts of the conversation. Pattern recognition across the session, and across sessions over time, is where AI adds qualitative value beyond what simple metrics can show.

**Fallback Behavior When AI Is Unavailable**
If the AI coaching note cannot be generated, the app shows only the quantitative metrics — which are calculated locally from the transcript and audio, not via AI. The feedback screen remains fully useful. The coaching note section shows: *"Coaching note unavailable for this session. Your metrics are still accurate and saved."*

The five core metrics are always calculated and always displayed. They are never dependent on an external AI service being available.

**Edge Cases**

- *Very short session (under 2 minutes)*: Metrics are displayed but accompanied by a note that shorter sessions produce less reliable trend data. The session is still saved and counted.
- *Session where the user barely spoke*: If the user's total speaking time is under 30 seconds, the feedback screen acknowledges this directly: *"It looks like this session was mostly listening. That's okay — try jumping in sooner next time. Here's what we could measure from what you did say."*
- *Filler word detection in names, technical terms, or non-English words*: The filler word list is curated to common English filler words only. Proper nouns, technical terminology, and foreign words are not counted as filler words regardless of their phonetic resemblance to filler words.
- *User disputes a metric*: There is a "This seems wrong" tap target next to each metric score. Tapping it allows the user to flag the metric as potentially inaccurate and submit the session for review. This is not a direct human review — it feeds into AI quality monitoring. A confirmation message explains this.

### Feature 3: Scenario Library

**What It Does**
The scenario library is a curated collection of speaking contexts, each with a distinct setup, AI behavior profile, and pressure tactics calibrated to what would actually happen in that real-world situation.

**Free tier scenarios (always available):**
1. General Impromptu Speaking — open-ended topics, moderate pressure, for warming up and building baseline comfort
2. Job Interview — Behavioral — STAR-format behavioral questions with impatient interviewer pressure
3. Classroom Presentation — peer and instructor questions, lower aggression, higher volume of "can you explain that more?" challenges

**Premium tier scenarios:**
4. Job Interview — Consulting/Finance — case-style and rapid-fire behavioral, impatient partner pressure
5. Investor Pitch — Seed/Series A — challenging assumptions, market size, competitive moat, founder conviction
6. Hostile Q&A — audience members who disagree, want to derail, or are playing devil's advocate
7. Difficult Conversations — delivering bad news, negotiating, addressing conflict
8. Debate Rounds — formal position defense with counter-argument pressure
9. Technical Explanation — explaining complex concepts to non-technical evaluators

New scenarios are added to the library on a monthly cadence. Users are notified when new scenarios relevant to their usage history are added.

**Progressive Unlocking**
Scenarios have recommended difficulty floors — a user who has never completed a session at difficulty 3 is gently advised not to start the Hostile Q&A scenario at difficulty 5. This is a recommendation, not a lock. Users can override it. The recommendation system prevents the discouragement of being overwhelmed in a first session — it does not create an artificial progression wall.

**Why It Matters**
A general-purpose AI pressure partner is valuable. A pressure partner that knows how a consulting partner behaves differently from a seed-stage VC — that knows a hostile Q&A crowd uses different tactics than a behavioral interviewer — is transformatively more useful. Scenario specificity is what converts practice into preparation. Marcus needs to practice for boardroom dynamics, not generic conversation. Elena needs investor-specific adversarial behavior, not generic follow-up questions.

**Where AI Enhances the Experience**
Each scenario has a prompt layer that shapes the AI's personality, tone, question types, and interruption style for that context. The AI playing a consulting partner speaks differently, interrupts differently, and challenges differently than the AI playing a seed-stage VC. This is not cosmetic — the specific pressure tactics of each context are what makes the practice transferable to the real situation.

**Fallback Behavior When AI Is Unavailable**
Each scenario has a curated static fallback library as described in Feature 1. The fallback content is scenario-specific — the fallback for Investor Pitch contains different questions than the fallback for Classroom Presentation.

**Edge Cases**

- *User selects a scenario far above recommended difficulty*: An advisory note appears on the scenario detail screen. The session starts normally if the user proceeds.
- *User wants to create a custom scenario*: Custom scenario building is a post-MVP feature noted on the roadmap. The settings screen has a "Request a scenario" option that allows users to submit scenario ideas.
- *Scenario content becomes outdated*: Scenario prompts are designed to be context-general rather than time-specific to minimize staleness. Date-specific content (e.g., references to current market conditions) is not included in scenario setup.

### Feature 4: Progress Tracking and Pattern Recognition

**What It Does**
The progress tracking system maintains a longitudinal record of the user's performance across all sessions. It tracks each of the five core metrics over time, identifies recurring patterns in when performance degrades, and surfaces insights that would not be visible from looking at individual session results.

The system distinguishes between overall improvement (the user's average scores improving over weeks) and situational patterns (the user performs well in opening remarks but degrades significantly under the first interruption in every session). Both dimensions of progress are shown.

**Why It Matters**
Speaking improvement is not linear, and it is not uniform across all conditions. A user might dramatically improve their filler word rate while their recovery time barely moves. A user might handle low-difficulty interruptions well but still fall apart at difficulty 4. Progress tracking that only shows aggregate scores obscures these patterns and denies the user the specific insight they need to direct their practice. Dev needs to see that he specifically falters when asked to explain without jargon — not just that his coherence score is 68%.

**Where AI Enhances the Experience**
The pattern insight engine reviews session data in aggregate to identify non-obvious correlations: performance at specific difficulty transitions, performance degradation at specific points in a session (e.g., always in the last five minutes), or performance that varies by scenario category in unexpected ways. These insights are surfaced as plain-language observations, not data tables.

**Edge Cases**

- *User has only one session*: No trend lines are shown. The progress screen acknowledges this and explains what will be visible after 3 sessions, after 10 sessions, and after 30 sessions.
- *Long gap between sessions (user returns after 2+ weeks)*: The app notes the gap without judgment: *"Welcome back. Here's where you were when you last practiced."* No shame language. No streak-breaking notifications.
- *Data export*: Users can export their full progress history as a structured data file from the Settings screen. This is available on both free and premium tiers.

### Feature 5: Ethical Billing and Subscription Management

**What It Does**
Cognitize's subscription system is fully managed within the app, with complete transparency at every step. Pricing is shown before trial start. Renewal dates and amounts are shown on the subscription management screen at all times. Cancellation is one tap from the subscription screen — it does not require navigating to a sub-menu, does not present a retention survey, and does not require contacting support.

The app sends a push notification (and email for users who have provided it) 3 days before any renewal. The notification states the exact renewal amount and date. It includes a direct link to the subscription management screen in the app.

Users who want to stop paying but not lose their streak and progress can pause their subscription for up to 60 days. Pausing maintains access to free tier features and preserves all progress data. This option is presented at the same level of prominence as cancellation.

**Why It Matters**
Dark patterns in subscription apps are a persistent and documented user harm. The competitor landscape for Cognitize is filled with apps that have earned 1- and 2-star reviews specifically because of surprise charges, buried cancellation flows, and misleading trial terms. These patterns do not just harm individual users — they poison the entire category. Any app in the speech coaching market that does ethical billing benefits from the low bar set by competitors.

More fundamentally: a speech coaching app is asking users to be vulnerable, to practice their weaknesses, to trust that the product will treat them fairly. Aggressive monetization is a direct contradiction of that relationship. Users who feel manipulated stop practicing. Ethical billing is not just the right thing to do — it is directly tied to the retention that makes a coaching product work.

**Edge Cases**

- *User attempts to cancel during trial*: No friction. Cancellation during trial immediately stops the subscription and produces a confirmation message: *"Your trial has been canceled. You won't be charged. You still have access to premium features until [date]."*
- *Subscription lapses due to payment failure*: User is notified immediately (push + email) with a clear, non-alarming message and a direct retry payment option. They retain premium access for 3 days while the payment issue is resolved. After 3 days without resolution, access gracefully downgrades to the free tier with all data preserved.
- *User in a jurisdiction with different consumer protection laws*: Billing behavior meets or exceeds the most protective standard applicable. There is no "race to the bottom" in compliance. Where regional law requires additional user protections (e.g., EU auto-renewal disclosure requirements), those protections are applied globally, not only where legally required.

## 5. What Must Be Different (Anti-Competitor Directive)

### Competitor Flaw 1: Passive Recording Masquerading as Coaching

**The Problem Users Face Today**
Every major competing app — Orai, Credible, Vocal Image — centers its core experience on the user speaking into a recording and then watching or reading a post-hoc analysis. This is measurement, not training. Users repeatedly report in reviews that they see the same issues flagged session after session without any improvement, because awareness of a problem is not the same as training a response to it. You cannot fix the way you speak under pressure by watching yourself speak without pressure.

**The Correct Behavior Cognitize Must Exhibit**
Every session must be interactive. There must be no passive recording mode. The AI must interrupt during the session, not after. The product experience must make the user feel the pressure of a real conversation — confusion, recovery, redirect — not the retrospective awareness of having not done it well enough.

**Why This Difference Is Critical**
The entire reason Cognitize can exist as a differentiated product is that it builds a skill that passive tools cannot build. If Cognitize ever introduces a "record and analyze" mode as a primary feature, it has surrendered its only unassailable advantage. The interruption engine is not a feature — it is the product.

---

### Competitor Flaw 2: Black-Box Scoring That Destroys User Trust

**The Problem Users Face Today**
Users of Vocal Image, Credible, and Orai consistently report that their scores change unexpectedly between sessions with no explanation, that "confidence ratings" are never defined, and that AI feedback sounds identical regardless of what they actually said. One user review of Credible reads: *"My confidence score went from 82 to 61 in two days and I have no idea why. I stopped trusting the app entirely."* When feedback cannot be trusted, the tool is useless regardless of its features.

**The Correct Behavior Cognitize Must Exhibit**
Every metric must be defined before the user encounters it. Every score must be explainable with reference to specific session data. If a score changes significantly between sessions, the feedback must explain why. The explanation must be specific — not *"your clarity improved"* but *"your filler word rate dropped from 6.2 to 3.8 per minute, driven by a reduction in 'um' usage during the Q&A portion of your session."*

There must be no metric in the app that the team cannot defend with a plain-language explanation of how it is calculated and what data it uses.

**Why This Difference Is Critical**
Trust is the product. A user who trusts their feedback practices consistently. A user who does not trust their feedback churns within two weeks. Transparent, explainable scoring is not just an ethical preference — it is the primary driver of long-term retention.

---

### Competitor Flaw 3: Paywalling Basic Functionality

**The Problem Users Face Today**
Orai's free tier is documented in user reviews as providing a single metric (filler words) and locking everything else — pacing, clarity, pitch — behind a subscription that begins at $9.99/month. Credible offers a free trial that gives access to premium features, then charges immediately when the trial ends without adequate notice. Multiple competitors surface paywall prompts within the first session — before the user has had any meaningful experience with the product. Users who encounter paywalls before experiencing value do not upgrade; they delete the app and leave angry reviews.

**The Correct Behavior Cognitize Must Exhibit**
The free tier must provide enough functionality to genuinely evaluate the core product. This means: multiple free scenarios, full feedback with all five metrics, session history visible, and at least 30 days of practice accessible without any subscription. A user who uses only the free tier for 30 days must come away feeling that Cognitize gave them real value — not that they were teased with a limited experience designed to coerce an upgrade.

Premium must add genuine new capabilities (additional scenario categories, advanced analytics, custom session lengths) — not unlock basic functionality that should have been free.

**Why This Difference Is Critical**
Paywalling basic functionality is a short-term conversion optimization that destroys long-term trust and organic growth. Every user who deletes the app because of a premature paywall is also a user who does not recommend it, does not leave a positive review, and potentially leaves a negative one. Cognitize's growth model depends on word-of-mouth from users who genuinely benefited — that only happens if the free tier is genuinely useful.

---

### Competitor Flaw 4: Hostile and Deceptive Subscription Practices

**The Problem Users Face Today**
Multiple competitors have documented histories of: auto-charging without adequate notice at trial end, burying cancellation in external support channels rather than in-app, presenting fake countdown timers and manufactured scarcity in upgrade flows, and using "downgrade" framing for cancellation to psychologically discourage it. Users who feel trapped by a subscription become angry brand detractors, not just churned customers.

**The Correct Behavior Cognitize Must Exhibit**
All subscription management must be entirely within the app. Cancellation must be reachable in three taps or fewer from the home screen at all times. Cancellation must produce an immediate, clear confirmation of what will happen next — including what features are retained and until when. There must be no retention surveys, no "are you sure?" multi-step confirmation, no emotional manipulation in the cancellation flow. Renewal reminders must be sent 3 days before every charge. Trial terms must be displayed in full before any payment information is entered.

**Why This Difference Is Critical**
Dark patterns in subscription management generate App Store reviews that explicitly warn other users not to download the app. They trigger Apple's App Review scrutiny and can result in rejection or removal from the App Store. They create legal exposure in jurisdictions with consumer protection law around auto-renewal. And fundamentally, they are incompatible with the trust relationship that a coaching product requires. Cognitize cannot ask users to be vulnerable in practice sessions and then betray their trust in the billing flow. These two things are directly contradictory.

---

### Competitor Flaw 5: Scenario Repetition and Practice Fatigue

**The Problem Users Face Today**
Public Speaking with AJ and similar apps offer a limited, static library of practice scenarios that users exhaust within weeks. Once you have heard the same ten prompts twenty times, practice becomes mechanical repetition rather than genuine challenge. The AI responses become predictable. The pressure disappears. The product stops working.

**The Correct Behavior Cognitize Must Exhibit**
The scenario library must be large enough at launch that repetition does not occur within the first 30 sessions. New scenarios must be added monthly. More importantly, the AI pressure engine must generate different interruptions and follow-up questions even within the same scenario — the unpredictability of the AI is itself a feature that prevents the habituation that kills practice value. No two sessions in the same scenario should feel identical, regardless of difficulty level.

**Why This Difference Is Critical**
Cognitize's core value proposition is building the ability to handle the unpredictable. An AI that becomes predictable defeats its own purpose. Session variety is not a content strategy decision — it is a functional requirement of the training method.

---

### Competitor Flaw 6: Generic, Non-Specific AI Feedback

**The Problem Users Face Today**
Users of AI coaching apps consistently report that the AI feedback sounds like it was written for everyone and no one. *"Speak more clearly."* *"Reduce filler words."* *"Maintain good pacing."* These are not insights — they are observations any listener could make. They do not tell the user when their filler word rate spikes, or why, or what triggers it. They do not identify that the user's pacing degrades specifically when challenged on numbers. Generic feedback has zero coaching value.

**The Correct Behavior Cognitize Must Exhibit**
Every piece of feedback must be traceable to specific moments in the specific session being reviewed. The coaching note must reference what happened in this session, not what happens generally to speakers with this profile. If the filler word detection shows a spike at minute 7, the feedback must note that the spike occurred at minute 7, and ideally what happened at minute 7 that might explain it (the AI had just delivered a challenging follow-up question). The goal is feedback that makes the user think *"yes, that is exactly what happened"* — not feedback that could have been written before they started speaking.

**Why This Difference Is Critical**
Specific feedback is the only feedback that produces behavioral change. Generic feedback produces awareness of a problem already known. Specific, moment-referenced feedback produces understanding of when and why the problem occurs — which is the prerequisite for training the problem away. The quality of Cognitize's feedback is what converts a user from someone who tried the app to someone who practices with it every day for six months.

## 6. Data & Content Requirements

### 6.1 Core Data Entities

**User Profile**
The central entity around which everything else orbits. Stores identity, onboarding state, current difficulty level, streak data, subscription status, and consent records. Every other entity in the app traces back to a user. The profile is the source of truth for what a user has access to, where they are in their progression, and whether their data can be synced to the cloud.

**Session**
The primary unit of activity in Cognitize. A session represents a single completed or abandoned pressure simulation — the scenario it used, when it started and ended, the difficulty level applied, and whether it was completed in full. Sessions are the backbone of all progress tracking and analytics. A session without feedback is incomplete data; a session without a transcript is unanalyzable. Both must be treated as first-class artifacts, not disposable byproducts.

**Transcript**
Each session produces a full transcript of everything the user said, with timing data attached. This is user-generated content — the raw output of their voice — and it must be treated accordingly: stored locally by default, only synced to the cloud with explicit user consent. Transcripts are the input to all feedback generation. They are also sensitive personal data. Losing a transcript means losing the ability to generate or regenerate feedback for that session.

**Interruption Log**
A record of every AI interruption that occurred during a session — what was said, when it was triggered relative to the transcript, and what type of interruption it was (follow-up question, topic pivot, challenge, etc.). This log is what allows the feedback system to contextualize the user's performance: were they flustered by a pivot? Did they recover well after an interruption? Without this log, feedback is generic. With it, feedback is situationally precise.

**Feedback Report**
The post-session analysis object. Contains scores across each feedback dimension (filler word frequency, pacing, clarity, response coherence, recovery from interruption, confidence markers), plain-language explanations for each score, and a comparison against the user's own historical baseline. Feedback reports are system-generated, not user-generated — but they reference user-generated data (transcript, interruption log) to produce their output. They must be permanently associated with their parent session and never mutable after generation.

**Scenario**
A structured speaking challenge with a defined context, a set of AI behavioral rules (how aggressive to be, what topics to introduce, what follow-up patterns to use), and a difficulty range. Scenarios are system-generated content curated by the Cognitize team. Users do not create scenarios in v1. Each scenario belongs to a category (job interview, investor pitch, impromptu speech, etc.) and has a progression tier that determines when it becomes available to a given user.

**Progress Snapshot**
A periodic summary object computed from a user's session history — rolling averages, trend lines, streak data, and improvement deltas across each feedback dimension. This is derived data, calculated from sessions and feedback reports. It powers the dashboard and progress views. It is never edited directly; it is always recomputed from source data.

**Subscription Record**
Tracks the user's current plan, billing cycle, next renewal date, and cancellation or pause state. This entity exists entirely to serve transparent billing — it is the source of truth displayed to the user at all times, not just at the point of purchase.

---

### 6.2 Content the App Creates, Stores, and Displays

**System-Generated Content**
- Scenario library entries (titles, descriptions, AI behavioral parameters, difficulty ratings)
- AI interruptions during sessions (generated in real time, logged post-session)
- Feedback reports (generated after each session, stored permanently)
- Progress snapshots (computed from session history on a rolling basis)
- Monthly new scenarios (added by the Cognitize team, not auto-generated)

**User-Generated Content**
- Session audio (captured during practice, stored locally by default)
- Transcripts (derived from audio, treated as user data with the same privacy weight as the audio itself)
- Any in-app notes a user chooses to attach to a session (future consideration, not v1)

**Content the App Displays**
- Live transcript text during a session (streaming, not buffered)
- AI interruption delivery (both text and voice)
- Post-session feedback report with scores, explanations, and historical comparisons
- Scenario catalog with unlock state and difficulty indicators
- Progress dashboard with trend charts and streak tracking
- Subscription status, renewal dates, and billing information — always visible, never buried

---

### 6.3 Information Architecture Principles

The app's information hierarchy is: **User → Sessions → [Transcripts + Interruption Logs + Feedback Reports]**. Everything else (scenarios, progress snapshots, subscription records) supports this core chain.

Content flows in one direction during a session: audio capture → live transcription → interruption generation → transcript logging. After a session, the direction reverses: transcript + interruption log → feedback generation → feedback report → progress snapshot update.

Progress data is always derived, never manually entered. If a user's session history changes (e.g., they delete a session), their progress snapshot must update to reflect the deletion. There is no concept of a manually inflated score or a synthetic achievement in Cognitize.

Scenario content is curated and versioned. When a scenario is updated, existing sessions referencing the old version of that scenario retain their original context — sessions are immutable records of what actually happened, not pointers to current scenario state.

## 7. Non-Functional Requirements

### 7.1 Performance — From the User's Perspective

**Interruption Latency**
The single most important performance requirement in the entire app. When the AI decides to interrupt, the user must hear or see that interruption within a perceptible instant of the trigger condition. Any delay greater than roughly a second shatters the illusion of a live, adversarial conversation partner. From the user's perspective, the interruption must feel like being cut off — not like a loading state. This is not a nice-to-have; it is the core product experience. If interruption latency is consistently bad, the app fails at its primary purpose regardless of how good everything else is.

**Session Start Time**
A user tapping "Start Session" must be in an active, live session within two seconds. Any longer and the cognitive preparation the user has built up begins to dissipate. The moment of starting a session is a commitment — the app must honor that commitment instantly.

**Feedback Report Load Time**
After a session ends, the feedback report should appear within ten seconds. Users are in a reflective state immediately post-session; that window is when feedback is most actionable. Delays push users away from the moment of peak receptivity. If the report takes longer due to AI processing, a progress indicator with specific status language ("Analyzing your pacing…", "Scoring your recovery moments…") is required — not a generic spinner.

**App Launch to Home Screen**
Cold launch to a usable home screen must complete within three seconds on a supported device. This is a baseline expectation for any production mobile app; Cognitize does not get an exception.

**Scrolling and Navigation**
All scrolling and screen transitions must be fluid. Janky list scrolling in the scenario library or progress history is not a minor aesthetic issue — it signals poor quality and erodes trust in the AI features that are harder to visually evaluate.

---

### 7.2 Accessibility Requirements

**Dynamic Type**
All text in the app must scale correctly with iOS Dynamic Type settings. Users who rely on larger text sizes — whether due to visual impairment or personal preference — must have a fully usable experience. This includes feedback reports, scenario descriptions, and live transcript text during sessions.

**VoiceOver Compatibility**
All interactive elements must have accurate, descriptive accessibility labels. Navigation controls, session controls, score indicators, and subscription management screens must all be operable via VoiceOver. The live session screen requires particular attention: interruption text and transcript updates must be surfaced as accessibility announcements, not silent visual updates.

**Color Contrast**
All text and meaningful UI elements must meet WCAG AA contrast standards at minimum. Score indicators that use color to convey meaning (e.g., a red score vs. a green score) must have a non-color fallback — an icon, a label, or a pattern — so the meaning is not lost for users with color vision deficiencies.

**Reduced Motion**
Any animations triggered by user action or AI events must respect the iOS "Reduce Motion" accessibility setting. The pressure simulation experience uses visual and audio cues to signal interruptions; the visual cues must degrade gracefully when motion reduction is active.

**Tap Target Sizes**
All interactive elements must meet Apple's Human Interface Guidelines minimum tap target size. This is especially critical for session control buttons that users interact with under real speaking pressure — they must be large enough to hit without breaking their train of thought.

---

### 7.3 Platform and Device Requirements

**Primary Target**
iOS, iPhone only at v1 launch. iPad is explicitly out of scope for v1. The interface is designed for one-handed and two-handed phone use during standing or seated speaking practice.

**Minimum iOS Version**
iOS 16 and above. This covers the vast majority of active iPhones and ensures access to modern audio APIs, system UI components, and security features without fragmentation concerns.

**Minimum Device**
iPhone SE (2nd generation) is the floor. The app must be performant and fully functional on this device. If a feature cannot run acceptably on an SE 2nd gen, it is either optimized until it can or it is deferred.

**Audio Hardware**
The app assumes the presence of a built-in microphone — it requires one to function. It must gracefully handle scenarios where microphone permission is denied, where a Bluetooth audio device is connected (and potentially introduce latency), or where the user switches audio routing mid-session. AirPods and standard wired headphones must work without degrading the pressure session experience.

**Orientation**
Portrait orientation only. The session screen and feedback report are designed for portrait. Forcing landscape or split-screen adds interface complexity that serves no meaningful user purpose in v1.

---

### 7.4 Offline Behavior

**What Must Work Without a Network Connection**
- Viewing previously completed sessions and their feedback reports
- Viewing the user's progress history and score trends
- Viewing the scenario library (content browsing, not starting a session)
- Viewing subscription and billing information

**What Requires a Network Connection**
- Starting a new pressure simulation session (real-time AI features require connectivity)
- Generating a new feedback report
- Syncing session data to the cloud (when the user has opted in)
- Subscription management actions (purchases, cancellations, pauses)

**Offline Handling**
When a user attempts to start a session without a network connection, the app must communicate this clearly — not with a generic error, but with explicit language explaining that the AI conversation partner requires a connection. The app must never silently fail, stall on a loading screen, or crash when connectivity is absent. All cached data (previous sessions, feedback reports, progress) must remain fully accessible offline without degradation.

**Data Resilience**
Session audio and transcripts are written to local storage first, before any cloud sync attempt. If a session completes but the feedback generation call fails due to a network drop, the transcript is preserved locally so feedback can be requested again when connectivity is restored. No session data should be permanently lost due to a transient network failure.

## 8. Success Criteria & Scope

### 8.1 North Star Metric

**Sessions completed per active user per week.**

This metric captures everything that matters about Cognitize in a single number. It requires the user to be active (not just downloaded), to find the scenarios worth returning to (not repetitive or unengaging), and to complete sessions (not abandon them due to poor UX, excessive latency, or demotivating feedback). A growing north star means the core pressure simulation is working and users are genuinely building a practice habit. A stagnating or declining north star is a signal that something fundamental is broken, regardless of what any other metric shows.

Target at 90 days post-launch: average of 2.5 completed sessions per active user per week.

---

### 8.2 Leading Indicators of Success

**Day-7 Retention**
The percentage of users who complete at least one session in their first week and return to complete another session in their second week. This is the earliest signal of whether the app has genuine habit-forming value. Target: 40% Day-7 retention among users who complete their first session.

**Session Completion Rate**
The percentage of started sessions that reach the natural end state rather than being abandoned mid-session. Abandonment is a direct signal that something in the pressure experience is wrong — either the AI is too aggressive for the difficulty level, the latency is intolerable, or the session structure is confusing. Target: 75% completion rate across all sessions in the first 30 days.

**Free-to-Premium Conversion Rate**
The percentage of free users who convert to a paid subscription. Because the free tier is intentionally generous, this metric measures whether the premium scenarios and analytics are genuinely desirable — not whether users are being paywalled into converting. A healthy conversion rate here means premium content is earning its price. A low rate may indicate the premium content needs strengthening, not that the free tier needs crippling. Target: 8–12% conversion within 60 days of first session.

**Feedback Report Engagement Rate**
The percentage of completed sessions where the user views their feedback report for more than 30 seconds. This measures whether the feedback is actually useful or whether users glance at a score and leave. Feedback that isn't read is feedback that isn't changing behavior. Target: 65% of sessions result in meaningful feedback engagement.

**Streak Retention at Day 14**
The percentage of users who maintain a practice streak of 3 or more days by the end of their second week. Streak behavior is a leading indicator of long-term retention. Target: 30% of active users maintaining a 3+ day streak at Day 14.

### 8.3 AI Quality Bars

These are the minimum acceptable performance thresholds below which the app is not ready for production. They are floors, not targets.

**Interruption Timing Accuracy**
At least 85% of interruptions must land at a contextually appropriate moment — mid-sentence, at a natural pause point, or after a triggerable phrase — as evaluated by a human reviewer on a random sample of 50 sessions. An interruption that fires before the user has said anything meaningful, or that triggers on random words with no contextual logic, breaks the experience entirely.

**Interruption Content Quality**
At least 90% of AI-generated interruptions must be topically relevant to what the user just said and appropriate to the scenario context. "Appropriate" means: not offensive, not factually absurd, not generic filler that could apply to any session. This is evaluated on the same 50-session random sample.

**Feedback Accuracy — Filler Word Count**
The AI's filler word count must match a human reviewer's manual count within ±10% on a random sample of 20 transcripts. Gross inaccuracies in filler word scoring will be noticed immediately by users and destroy trust in all other scores.

**Feedback Accuracy — Pacing Score**
The pacing score must reflect actual words-per-minute variance in a way that a non-technical user would recognize as correct. A session where the user spoke at a calm, steady pace must not receive a "rushed" pacing score. This is evaluated qualitatively against 20 test sessions with known pacing profiles.

**Feedback Report Generation Reliability**
At least 98% of completed sessions must successfully generate a feedback report within 30 seconds of session completion. Failures must surface a clear, honest error state with a retry option — never a silent void.

**Content Safety**
Zero tolerance for AI-generated interruptions that are offensive, discriminatory, or harmful in any form. The content safety layer must block 100% of test cases containing known violation categories before they reach the user.

### 8.5 Open Questions for Development

1. **Interruption trigger logic**: What is the precise behavioral model for when the AI interrupts? Is it time-based, speech-pattern-based, keyword-triggered, or some combination? The answer determines the realism of the pressure experience and needs explicit definition before the session engine is built.

2. **Difficulty calibration methodology**: How is a new user's starting difficulty determined during onboarding? Is it a short diagnostic session, a self-report questionnaire, or a hybrid? The onboarding experience sets the first impression — getting calibration wrong (too hard or too easy) has an outsized impact on Day-1 retention.

3. **Feedback dimension weighting**: Are all feedback dimensions (filler words, pacing, clarity, coherence, confidence, interruption recovery) weighted equally in an overall score, or is there a weighted composite? Transparent scoring requires this to be defined and disclosed to users — not decided arbitrarily post-launch.

4. **Session abandonment handling**: If a user stops speaking mid-session and doesn't formally end it, what happens? Is there a silence timeout? Does the AI prompt them? Does the session auto-close and generate partial feedback? The edge case behavior here affects data integrity and user experience.

5. **Scenario unlock criteria**: What specific conditions must a user meet to unlock higher-difficulty scenarios? Is it a number of completed sessions, a minimum score threshold, a combination, or time-based? The unlock logic directly affects the "non-punitive progression" promise.

6. **Premium trial behavior**: Does the premium tier offer a free trial? If so, what is the trial length, what happens at the end of the trial, and how is the 3-day renewal reminder handled for trial expirations versus recurring renewals?

7. **Transcript deletion and privacy**: If a user deletes a session, what happens to the associated transcript, feedback report, and the contribution of that session to their progress snapshot? The deletion behavior must be clearly defined and must align with GDPR/CCPA obligations.

## 9. Anti-Patterns

### 9.1 Things That Must Not Be Built

**A Paywall on Core Functionality**
The entire value proposition of Cognitize's monetization rests on the promise that the core experience — pressure simulation, honest feedback, progress tracking — is genuinely free. Building a free tier that only allows one session, or that withholds feedback scores, or that shows a paywall after two minutes of use, is not a "business decision." It is a betrayal of the product's stated identity and will be called out immediately by users who have been burned by every competitor doing exactly this. The free tier must be generous enough that a user can meaningfully evaluate whether this app is worth paying for.

**Black-Box or Unchallengeable Scores**
Displaying a score without explaining how it was calculated is an anti-pattern Cognitize explicitly exists to correct. Never show a user a "7.2/10 speaking confidence score" without immediately telling them, in plain language, what inputs produced that number and what would change it. Mystery scores feel authoritative but aren't trustworthy — and trust is the entire foundation of a feedback product.

**Artificial Score Inflation to Drive Engagement**
Do not design the feedback system to make users feel good. Design it to make users better. Inflated scores to prevent churn, feel-good messages that obscure real weaknesses, and "great job!" responses to genuinely poor sessions are patronizing and useless. The competitive research shows users recognize and resent this pattern. Honest feedback — delivered with appropriate framing — is the product. Flattery is the anti-product.

**Aggressive Re-engagement Notifications**
Push notifications that pressure users to "keep their streak alive!" every day, shame them for missed sessions, or bombard them with marketing messages disguised as coaching prompts will destroy trust and drive uninstalls. Cognitize is building a practice habit tool, not a notification addiction loop. Notifications must be opt-in, infrequent, and always directly relevant to the user's own stated goals.

**Subscription Dark Patterns**
This is a hard line, not a guideline. Never: obscure the cancellation path, require a support ticket or phone call to cancel, hide the renewal date, charge a user who thought they cancelled, continue charging after a user has paused, or require the user to navigate away from the app to manage their subscription. Every one of these patterns has appeared in competitors and has generated the one-star reviews that created the market gap Cognitize is filling. Any deliberate introduction of these patterns is a product failure, regardless of any short-term revenue impact.

**"Gamification" That Trivializes the Practice**
Streaks and progress indicators are useful when they reflect real improvement. Badges for "completing your first session," confetti animations for hitting a score, or leaderboards that rank users against strangers trivialize what is genuinely challenging, personal work. Cognitize's users are preparing for high-stakes situations — job interviews, investor pitches, public presentations. The app must treat that seriousness with respect. Light progress reinforcement is acceptable; turning speech coaching into a points-accumulation game is not.

**Scope Creep Toward Passive Features**
Cognitize's entire differentiation is that it is *active and adversarial*, not passive and observational. Any feature that moves the product toward "record yourself and listen back" territory — without interactive AI challenge — is a step toward becoming exactly what Cognitize is designed to displace. Every new feature proposal must answer the question: does this increase active pressure, or does it reduce it?

### 9.2 Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**Letting Latency Creep in Silently**
Interruption latency will degrade over time as the AI pipeline accumulates complexity, context windows grow, and infrastructure load increases. This degradation is invisible in sprint-by-sprint development but catastrophic to the product experience. Latency must be measured and monitored continuously — not just tested at launch. If interruption latency exceeds acceptable thresholds, it is a P1 issue, not a backlog item.

**Overbuilding the Scenario Library Before the Engine Is Proven**
Ten mediocre scenarios built on a shaky pressure engine are worth less than five great scenarios built on a solid one. The scenario library is only as valuable as the quality of AI interaction within each scenario. Do not expand breadth at the expense of depth. The launch library of five scenarios must each be genuinely good before any new scenarios are added.

**Treating Feedback as a Post-Processing Afterthought**
The feedback report is not a receipt. It is the product's second most important moment, after the session itself. Rushing feedback design, treating it as a data dump of scores, or delegating its language to generic AI output without editorial control will produce a feature that users open once and never return to. Feedback copy — the plain-language explanations of every score — must be written with care and tested with real users.

**Ignoring Edge Cases in the Session Flow**
What happens when the user gets a phone call mid-session? When they close the app and reopen it? When they lose network connectivity after starting a session? When the microphone permission is revoked while a session is active? Each of these scenarios is a potential crash, data loss event, or user trust violation. They must be identified and explicitly handled — not discovered by users in production.

**Building Premium Features Before Free Features Are Excellent**
The conversion path from free to premium requires users who trust the product. That trust is built entirely in the free tier. Building advanced premium scenarios before the free scenarios are polished and reliable is backwards. Make the free experience so good that paying for more feels obvious — not obligatory.

**Mistaking Downloads for Validation**
An early spike in downloads — from press coverage, social media, or App Store featuring — is not validation that the product works. Validation is a user completing three sessions in their first week and returning for more. Track retention and session completion from day one, and do not let download numbers obscure what is actually happening with engagement.
