# Cognitize: Product Brief for AI Development Agent

---

## 1. Vision & Mission

### What This App Is

Cognitize is an AI-powered speech coaching app that actively puts users under conversational pressure during live sessions — interrupting them mid-sentence, firing pointed follow-up questions, and introducing unexpected topic pivots — then delivers transparent, evidence-based feedback on what happened and why. It is the only speech tool built around adversarial interaction rather than passive recording and playback.

The app serves professionals, students, founders, and anyone who knows they crumble under real speaking pressure and wants a safe place to get harder before the stakes are real.

### Who It Serves

The primary user is an adult aged 18–40, iOS-first, who has an upcoming high-stakes speaking situation — a job interview, a board presentation, an investor pitch, a difficult conversation — and recognizes that rehearsing in front of a mirror or recording themselves talking into a phone does not replicate the pressure of being challenged in real time. They are motivated, slightly anxious about speaking, and deeply skeptical of apps that oversell and underdeliver.

Secondary users include students building baseline public speaking confidence, coaches who recommend tools to clients, and power users who treat ongoing pressure simulation as a skill-maintenance habit the way athletes treat drills.

### Why This App Must Exist

Public speaking anxiety affects an estimated 75% of the population. Despite this, the app market offers two inadequate options: passive recording tools that let users listen back to themselves (useful but low-impact), and language-learning apps that build vocabulary but don't address the physiological and cognitive experience of being challenged mid-thought.

The core problem no existing tool solves is this: real speaking pressure is interactive and unpredictable. A tough interviewer doesn't wait for you to finish your answer before they push back. A skeptical investor doesn't let you deliver your pitch uninterrupted. The skill that breaks down under pressure — and that most needs practice — is the ability to think, speak, and recover in real time when someone disrupts your flow. No current app trains this. Cognitize is built entirely around it.

### Core Market Insight

The gap is not in recording quality, AI accuracy, or lesson content. The gap is in adversarial interactivity. Users who are already decent speakers in low-stakes environments need to experience pressure and fail safely, repeatedly, until pressure stops triggering cognitive shutdown. Cognitize's entire product philosophy is: failure is the training mechanism, not a sign the app isn't working.

### Evidence From Competitor Analysis

- **Orai** (closest direct competitor): User complaints cluster around inaccurate AI feedback, aggressive paywall placement on features users expected to be free, and — critically — the complete absence of any interruption or real-time challenge. Users report practicing the same polished delivery repeatedly with no simulation of being disrupted.
- **Vocal Image**: High ratings (4.53/5) but six distinct UX flaws identified including opaque scoring, unclear what metrics mean, and no adversarial interaction. Good at measuring; bad at training.
- **Credible**: Four identified competitor flaws including passive session model and aggressive monetization friction before users see core value.
- **Public Speaking with AJ**: Best-rated in competitive set (4/5, fewest flaws) but constrained by a human coach bottleneck — not scalable, not available on-demand, and not adversarial in an AI-driven way.
- **Across all competitors**: Zero apps in the competitive set offer real-time AI interruption during a live session. This is the moat. It is currently unoccupied.

## 2. User Personas & Their Problems

---

### Persona 1: Marcus — The Career Climber

**Who He Is**
Marcus is 28, a mid-level marketing manager at a technology company. He's technically competent, well-liked, and preparing for a director-level promotion that requires him to present to the C-suite quarterly. His peers view him as sharp in one-on-ones but visibly shaky in high-stakes group settings. He knows this about himself and it frustrates him deeply.

**His Context**
He has a presentation in six weeks. He's tried recording himself on his phone, practiced in front of his partner, and watched YouTube videos on vocal presence. None of it has replicated the feeling of being in the room, and he knows it. He downloaded Orai two months ago, used it three times, hit the paywall on the feature he actually wanted, and deleted it.

**Frustrations With Existing Solutions**
- Recording tools show him what he sounds like but don't train him to think under pressure
- Paywall appeared before he could tell if the app was worth his money
- AI feedback felt generic — told him he used "um" 14 times but didn't help him understand when or why
- No simulation of the moment his boss cuts him off mid-slide and asks a hostile question
- Progress felt invisible — he couldn't tell if he was improving

**What Success Looks Like**
Marcus completes a 10-minute simulated C-suite pressure session, gets cut off three times with pointed questions, stumbles twice, recovers once, and receives a specific, timestamped breakdown of where his pacing collapsed and what his filler word pattern reveals about his anxiety spikes. Six weeks later, he walks into the real presentation with documented evidence that he has survived harder sessions than this one.

**Day-in-the-Life Scenario**
It's 7:15am. Marcus has 20 minutes before his commute. He opens Cognitize, selects "Executive Presentation" from his saved scenarios, sets difficulty to Level 4 (his current calibrated level), and starts a session. At minute 2, mid-explanation of a market strategy, the AI interrupts: "Hold on — you said 'significant growth' twice. Define significant. Give me a number." Marcus falters, recovers with a specific figure, continues. The AI interrupts again at minute 4 with a topic pivot. The session ends. His post-session dashboard shows filler word count down from his personal baseline of 18 to 11, pacing variance flagged at minute 3, and a specific callout: "Your response to the numerical challenge at 2:14 showed a 4-second pause before recovery — your best recovery time this week." He screenshots this and saves it. He feels measurably better.

### Persona 2: Priya — The Pitch-Ready Founder

**Who She Is**
Priya is 34, a SaaS founder preparing for a Series A raise. She has pitched six angel investors with mixed results. Post-pitch feedback is vague — "work on your story" — and she has no reliable way to replicate the specific pressure of a hostile investor Q&A. She is disciplined, data-driven, and willing to pay for tools that demonstrably work.

**Her Context**
She has three VC meetings in the next 30 days. Her co-founder tells her she rushes when challenged. She agrees but doesn't know how to fix it because she has no training environment that replicates being challenged. She's tried practicing with friends but they're too supportive to push back realistically.

**Frustrations With Existing Solutions**
- No tool offers a hostile investor simulation — the specific scenario she needs most
- Recording her pitch and playing it back doesn't help her respond to "Why would anyone pay for this when Salesforce could build it in a quarter?"
- Speech coaching services cost $300/hour and can't be done at 6am before her kids wake up
- Apps she's tried feel like toys — nothing feels like the real stakes of being challenged

**What Success Looks Like**
Priya runs the "Hostile Investor Q&A" scenario at Level 6 pressure every evening for two weeks before her first VC meeting. The AI fires the ten most common objection patterns investors use, interrupts her mid-answer with "You didn't answer my question," and tracks her coherence scores over 14 sessions. By the day of the pitch, she has answered every major objection type under pressure at least eight times. Her clarity score on objection responses has moved from 58 to 79 over two weeks. She walks into the meeting having failed, recovered, and improved in a closed environment — which is exactly what she needed.

**Day-in-the-Life Scenario**
It's 10pm. Priya has 25 minutes. She selects "Investor Q&A — Hostile Mode" (a premium scenario), difficulty Level 6. The AI plays the role of a skeptical partner-level VC. She delivers her opening. At 45 seconds the AI cuts in: "What's your churn?" She answers. "That's higher than your competitors — why would we fund this?" She pushes back. The AI escalates: "You're not answering the question, you're deflecting." Post-session: her response coherence on objection handling scored 71, up from 64 last session. Three specific moments flagged with timestamps where she deflected instead of answered. She reviews those three clips, notes a pattern — she rushes to reframe instead of validating the concern first — and sets a personal focus note for tomorrow's session.

When they eventually try moot court practice, they've already survived dozens of adversarial interruptions in a private environment and their cognitive shutdown response is measurably reduced.

Devon screenshots the note and adds it to their moot court prep notes.

A trajectory. She sets a weekly session goal and comes back Thursday.

## 3. Core User Experiences (Screen-by-Screen)

---

### Screen 1: Onboarding — Welcome & Value Proposition

**Purpose**
First impression. Communicates what makes Cognitize categorically different from every other speech app the user has tried and establishes trust before asking for anything.

**What Users See**
Three to four onboarding slides presented sequentially. Slide 1 states the core value proposition directly and plainly — something like "The AI that interrupts you. On purpose." — with a brief explanation of why interruption is the training mechanism. Slide 2 shows a visual representation of what a pressure session looks like (waveform with interruption markers, post-session feedback). Slide 3 is an explicit, honest breakdown of what's free and what's paid — presented as a table or clear list, not buried text. Slide 4 is an optional 3-question calibration survey (speaking experience level, primary use case, upcoming event if any).

**All States**
- *Default*: Sequential swipeable cards with progress dots. Skip available at all times.
- *Calibration survey skipped*: App notes that calibration is skipped and starts user at Level 2 (conservative default). User can recalibrate anytime in settings.
- *No internet connection*: Onboarding cards load fully from local cache. Calibration survey still works; data syncs when connection restores.

**Interactions**
- Swiping or tapping "Next" advances cards
- Tapping "Skip" at any point jumps directly to account creation
- Calibration answers directly set the starting difficulty level and the initial scenario recommendations shown on the home screen
- "See full pricing" link on the pricing slide opens a modal with complete tier breakdown — not an external web link

**Navigation**
Onboarding → Account Creation (or Guest Mode selection)

---

### Screen 2: Account Creation & Authentication

**Purpose**
Get the user into the app with minimal friction while collecting enough information for personalized progress tracking.

**What Users See**
Options for email/password sign-up, Sign in with Apple, and a clearly labeled "Try Without Account" guest mode option. Guest mode is not hidden or de-emphasized. Below the sign-up options, a one-line statement: "We do not sell your data. Audio stays on your device unless you opt into cloud sync." This statement is visible before account creation, not after.

**All States**
- *Default*: Sign-up options presented equally
- *Email validation error*: Inline error below the email field (not a modal interruption)
- *Password too weak*: Real-time strength indicator, plain-language requirement text
- *Existing account detected*: Prompt to sign in instead, with option to reset password
- *Guest mode*: User proceeds with local-only storage. A non-intrusive banner appears in the home screen noting that progress won't sync across devices, with a one-tap option to create an account anytime. No repeated prompts — one mention per session at most.
- *No internet*: Email sign-up disabled with explanation. Apple Sign-In may work offline depending on device state. Guest mode always available.

**Interactions**
- Tapping "Try Without Account" immediately bypasses this screen entirely and lands on home
- Sign-up completion triggers a brief (5-second) welcome animation and then proceeds to home
- New users who signed up via Apple are asked for a display name on first home screen visit since Apple obscures real names

**Navigation**
Account Creation → Home Screen

---

### Screen 3: Home Screen — Session Hub

**Purpose**
The central navigation point. Users should be able to start a session within two taps from here. Everything else is secondary.

**What Users See**
A primary "Start Session" call-to-action button, visually dominant, at the top of the screen. Below it: the user's last session summary (scenario name, date, top metric — e.g., "Filler words: 8 — your best this week"). Below that: a "Continue where you left off" card if a scenario is in progress. Below that: a horizontally scrollable row of scenario recommendations based on calibration data and recent activity. At the bottom: navigation to Scenario Library, Progress Dashboard, and Settings.

**All States**
- *First-time user (no sessions)*: Instead of "last session," a welcoming prompt: "Run your first session — it takes 3 minutes." Scenario recommendations show the three free starter scenarios.
- *Returning user*: Full summary of last session visible. Streak indicator if they have one (minimum 3 consecutive days to show streak — not gamified aggressively).
- *Guest mode*: A persistent but quiet indicator that they're in guest mode, with a single non-intrusive account creation prompt. Progress visible but locally only.
- *Premium content visible to free users*: Premium scenarios appear in recommendation row with a clear "Premium" label — not blurred, not locked with a padlock icon that implies shame. A brief one-line note: "Included in Cognitize Pro."
- *Offline*: "Offline mode" indicator in header. "Start Session" still works for pre-loaded scenarios using local speech processing. Cloud-dependent features (progress sync, premium scenarios) show a "Requires connection" note inline.

**Interactions**
- "Start Session" opens the Session Setup screen with the last-used scenario pre-selected
- Tapping a scenario recommendation card opens its detail page
- Pull-to-refresh updates session history and recommendations
- Tapping the streak indicator opens the Progress Dashboard

**Navigation**
Home → Session Setup, Scenario Library, Progress Dashboard, Settings, Scenario Detail

---

### Screen 4: Session Setup

**Purpose**
Let the user configure and launch a session with confidence — the right scenario, the right difficulty, the right duration. No friction; no decision fatigue.

**What Users See**
Scenario name and a two-to-three sentence plain-English description of what the AI will do in this scenario — not marketing language, but behavioral description (e.g., "The AI will play a skeptical hiring manager. It will interrupt you 2–4 times per minute at this difficulty and ask follow-up questions challenging your specific claims."). Below: a difficulty slider with 8 levels, the user's recommended level pre-selected based on history. Each level has a one-line behavioral description (e.g., Level 3: "One interruption every 90 seconds, follow-up questions on main claims only"). Below: session duration selector (3 min / 5 min / 10 min / custom). A microphone permission status indicator — if permission hasn't been granted, a contextual request appears here, not at app launch. At the bottom: a large "Begin Session" button.

**All States**
- *Microphone permission not granted*: "Begin Session" is replaced with "Allow Microphone Access" — tapping it triggers the native permission dialog. If the user denies, a plain-English explanation of why it's needed with a link to device settings appears. No guilt-tripping language.
- *Premium scenario, free user*: The setup screen is visible and browseable but "Begin Session" becomes "Unlock with Pro" with a one-tap path to the subscription screen. The scenario description is fully readable — users can decide if it's worth upgrading before committing.
- *Low battery (under 20%)*: A small notice: "Low battery — consider charging before a long session to avoid interruption."
- *Poor network connection*: "Limited connectivity detected. Your session will use on-device processing. Some AI features may be reduced." User can proceed or wait.
- *Custom duration selected*: A numeric input field appears. Min: 2 minutes. Max: 30 minutes. Values outside this range are auto-corrected with a brief explanation.

**Interactions**
- Difficulty slider change updates the behavioral description text in real time
- Tapping the scenario name opens the scenario detail page in a bottom sheet (without leaving setup)
- "Begin Session" with all permissions granted transitions immediately to the live session screen — no loading screen between setup and session start

**Navigation**
Session Setup → Live Session Screen (or Subscription Screen if premium gated)

---

### Screen 5: Live Session Screen

**Purpose**
The core experience. Everything about this screen is designed to keep the user in the speaking mindset — not thinking about the UI. The screen should fade into the background so the user can focus entirely on speaking.

**What Users See**
A clean, dark-mode-default screen (reduces visual distraction). A large waveform visualizer showing the user's live audio input — confirming the mic is active and picking up their voice. A session timer counting up. A discreet indicator showing the current scenario and difficulty. When the AI speaks (interruptions, questions), a distinct visual indicator pulses to show AI is speaking — different color from user waveform. The AI's spoken words appear as scrolling text subtitles in real time as it speaks. A "Pause" button in the corner, accessible but not prominent. No score indicators, no progress bars, no gamification elements during the session — these appear only in post-session feedback.

**All States**
- *User speaking*: Waveform animates with input. No AI indicators active.
- *AI speaking (interruption/question)*: AI waveform indicator activates. User waveform mutes visually (though microphone continues to pick up audio for transcription continuity). AI subtitles appear.
- *AI processing (latency gap between user speech and AI response)*: A subtle breathing animation on the AI indicator shows "thinking." If this exceeds 3 seconds, a brief text note: "Thinking..." This never appears as a loading spinner or error.
- *User silent for more than 10 seconds*: A gentle prompt appears: "Take your time — or say 'skip' to move on." At 20 seconds silence, a second prompt: "The AI will move to the next question in 10 seconds." This is informational, not pressuring.
- *Session paused*: Screen overlays a dimmed pause state. Session timer stops. A "Resume" button and an "End Session Early" option are visible. No confirmation dialog for resume — single tap resumes immediately.
- *Offline mid-session*: If internet drops during an active session, the app switches to local processing mode without interrupting the session. A small banner: "Offline — using local processing. AI complexity reduced." The session continues. Post-session feedback will be partially reduced but still delivered.
- *Microphone input drops out*: A visible alert: "Microphone input interrupted. Check your connection and tap to resume." Session pauses automatically. No data lost — transcript preserved to that point.
- *Session ends (timer reaches selected duration)*: A brief audio chime and fade animation. The live screen transitions automatically to the post-session feedback screen.

**Interactions**
- Tapping "Pause" pauses session and timer
- Saying "skip" or "next question" out loud advances the AI to its next interruption point (voice command recognized by the transcription layer)
- Tapping the waveform area has no effect — prevents accidental taps from disrupting session
- Swiping down on the session screen triggers a confirmation modal: "End this session? Your progress so far will still be saved and scored."

**Navigation**
Live Session → Post-Session Feedback (automatic on session end) or Home (on manual early exit after confirmation)

### Screen 6: Post-Session Feedback Screen

**Purpose**
Deliver honest, specific, non-generic feedback that gives the user actionable insight and visible evidence of their progress. This is the highest-value screen in the app — it must justify the effort of the session and pull users back for the next one.

**What Users See**
A header with the session scenario, date, duration, and difficulty level. Below: a "Session Score" — a single composite number (0–100) displayed prominently, with a visible one-line explanation of what that number means and exactly what went into it (e.g., "Your score combines filler word rate (30%), pacing consistency (25%), response coherence (30%), and recovery speed (15%)"). Below the composite score: four individual metric cards — one per sub-score — each showing the raw number, its contribution to total score, and a plain-language sentence explaining what it means. Each metric card shows the user's personal trend line for that metric over their last five sessions. Below the metrics: a timestamped moments section — a list of specific moments in the session with timestamps, each labeled as either a "Strength" or a "Challenge," with a one-to-two sentence explanation of what happened and why it was flagged. These moments are playable — users can tap a timestamp to hear their own audio clip from that moment. At the bottom: a "Focus for Next Session" recommendation — a single specific suggestion derived from the session's weakest metric (e.g., "Your recovery time after challenges is your current gap. In your next session, try the 'Challenge Recovery' drill at Level 3.").

**All States**
- *Session too short (under 90 seconds)*: Score is not generated. A message: "Sessions under 90 seconds don't generate complete feedback. Here's what we captured — run a full session for a complete score."
- *Offline session (reduced AI features)*: Feedback is still delivered but clearly labeled: "This session used on-device processing. Coherence scoring is unavailable offline. Filler words, pacing, and recovery time are accurate." No fake scores for missing metrics.
- *First session ever*: No trend lines yet. Metrics display as baseline anchors with a note: "This is your baseline — every future session will be compared to this."
- *Personal best achieved on any metric*: A discreet, non-gamified callout: "Your best filler word rate in 30 days." No confetti, no excessive celebration — just a clear factual note.
- *All metrics green for user's history*: A single plain sentence: "Your strongest session this month." No over-inflation of what this means.
- *Feedback loading (processing after session end)*: A progress indicator with stage labels: "Analyzing transcript... Calculating coherence... Preparing your feedback." Never shows a generic "Loading..." — always shows what's happening and why it takes a moment. Target: under 15 seconds.

**Interactions**
- Tapping a metric card expands it to show the full trend graph and a one-paragraph explanation of what drives that metric and how to improve it
- Tapping a timestamped moment plays the audio clip from that moment in a bottom sheet player
- "Share Session Summary" generates a clean image card of the key metrics (no audio included) — user controls whether to share or not; no auto-sharing
- "Start Next Session" pre-loads the same scenario at the same or adjusted difficulty based on performance
- "Save to History" is automatic — but users see a confirmation that their session has been saved locally (and to cloud if sync enabled)

**Navigation**
Post-Session Feedback → Home, Scenario Library, or directly into another session

### Screen 7: Scenario Library

**Purpose**
Let users discover, preview, and select speaking scenarios. Browseable without commitment. Premium scenarios visible but not hidden — transparent about what's behind the upgrade.

**What Users See**
A searchable, filterable grid or list of scenarios organized into categories: Job Interviews, Investor Pitches, Academic & Classroom, Difficult Conversations, Impromptu Speaking, Debate & Argumentation. Each scenario card shows: scenario name, category tag, difficulty range (e.g., "Levels 2–7"), a two-sentence description of the AI behavior in that scenario, a "Free" or "Pro" label, and if the user has completed it before, their last score and date. A filter bar at the top allows filtering by category, difficulty range, and free/all. A "Recommended for you" section at the top shows two to three scenarios based on calibration data and recent activity.

**All States**
- *No sessions completed*: All scenario cards show without personal history. A callout: "Complete your first session to unlock personalized recommendations."
- *Scenario locked (premium, free user)*: Scenario card is fully visible and readable — not blurred. A "Pro" badge. Tapping it opens the scenario detail page which includes a full description and a non-aggressive "Available in Cognitize Pro" section at the bottom.
- *New scenario added this month*: A "New" badge on the card for 30 days after addition
- *Search returns no results*: "No scenarios match this search. New scenarios are added monthly — check back soon." No empty-state failure messaging.
- *Offline*: Scenarios the user has accessed before are available from cache. New scenarios marked "Requires connection to load for the first time."

**Interactions**
- Tapping a scenario card opens Scenario Detail (bottom sheet or new screen)
- Long-pressing a scenario card adds it to a personal "Practice Queue" — a saved list for quick access
- Filter selections persist across app sessions

**Navigation**
Scenario Library → Scenario Detail → Session Setup

### Screen 8: Scenario Detail

**Purpose**
Give the user enough context to confidently decide to run a session with this scenario — what the AI will do, what difficulty feels like, what they'll practice.

**What Users See**
Scenario name, category, a full-paragraph description of the scenario context (e.g., "You're presenting a Series A pitch to a partner at a top-tier VC firm. They are skeptical of your market sizing and have limited patience for vague answers. They will interrupt you, challenge specific claims, and ask follow-up questions that require precise, confident responses."). Below: a difficulty guide — what each level feels like in this specific scenario. Below: a "What you'll practice" list (three to five plain-English skill areas). Below: a "Sample interruptions" section showing two to three example interruptions the AI might use in this scenario — so users know what to expect and aren't blindsided the first time. Below: the user's history with this scenario if they've run it before (last score, number of sessions, trend). At the bottom: "Start This Scenario" button.

**All States**
- *Premium scenario, free user*: Full detail visible. "Start This Scenario" replaced with "Available in Cognitize Pro" — with a non-pressuring, single-tap path to the subscription screen
- *No history with this scenario*: History section replaced with "First time here — your baseline will be set on your first session"
- *Scenario recently updated*: A note at the top: "Updated this month — new question set added"

**Interactions**
- "Start This Scenario" navigates to Session Setup with this scenario pre-selected
- Tapping a sample interruption plays a brief audio preview of what the AI sounds like delivering it

**Navigation**
Scenario Detail → Session Setup

### Screen 9: Progress Dashboard

**Purpose**
Show users concrete evidence that they are improving — or honestly show them where they're plateauing. Non-judgmental, data-driven, and benchmarked only against the user's own history.

**What Users See**
At the top: a headline metric — "X sessions this month" and a brief trend statement (e.g., "Your average session score is up 12 points over the past 4 weeks"). Below: a full-width trend graph for composite session score over time, with selectable time windows (7 days, 30 days, 90 days, all time). Below the main graph: four individual metric trend graphs (filler words, pacing, coherence, recovery time) each independently selectable. Below: a "Skills Breakdown" section showing current estimated level for each core skill, with plain-English descriptions of what each level means behaviorally. Below: a "Recent Sessions" list with quick-access links to individual post-session feedback for any prior session. At the bottom: a "Hardest moment this month" callout — the single most challenging interruption moment the user faced, with a timestamp link to the audio.

**All States**
- *Fewer than 3 sessions completed*: Trend graphs show available data points but include a note: "More sessions give you more accurate trend data — aim for 3 sessions per week." Not a nag; a context setter.
- *User has plateaued (less than 2% change across 4+ sessions)*: A callout: "You've been consistent at this level for 4 sessions. Consider increasing difficulty to keep progressing." Direct, not alarming.
- *No sessions in 14 days*: A gentle re-engagement note at the top of the dashboard — one instance only, not repeated.
- *Guest mode*: Full dashboard visible with local data. A note at the top: "Your progress is saved on this device only. Create an account to access it across devices."

**Interactions**
- Tapping any session in "Recent Sessions" opens its post-session feedback screen
- Tapping a metric trend graph opens an expanded view with more detail
- Time window selector changes all visible graphs simultaneously

**Navigation**
Progress Dashboard → Post-Session Feedback (individual session history), Settings

### Screen 10: Subscription & Billing Screen

**Purpose**
Present pricing completely honestly, in full, before any payment is requested. No dark patterns, no hidden terms, no post-trial surprise charges. This screen is a trust-building experience, not a conversion funnel.

**What Users See**

### Feature 1: Pressure Simulation Engine

**What It Does**
The Pressure Simulation Engine is the live AI conversation partner that runs during every session. It listens to the user speak in real time, detects natural pause points and high-confidence content segments, and fires adversarial interventions: interruptions that cut the user off mid-thought, pointed follow-up questions challenging specific claims the user just made, and topic pivots that shift the conversation without warning. The AI speaks its interventions out loud in a human-sounding voice and displays them as text simultaneously. The user must respond in real time — there is no "waiting for them to be ready."

The engine is calibrated by difficulty level (1–8). At low levels (1–2), interruptions are infrequent, follow-up questions are predictable, and the AI does not cut users off mid-sentence — it waits for natural pauses. At mid levels (3–5), interruptions become mid-sentence, questions become more pointed and reference specific claims the user made, and occasional topic pivots are introduced. At high levels (6–8), the AI interrupts frequently, challenges coherence directly ("You didn't answer my question"), escalates if the user deflects, and maintains the persona of a genuinely skeptical interlocutor.

**Why It Matters**
This is the only feature that directly trains the skill that breaks down under pressure: thinking and speaking coherently when disrupted. Recording tools train polish; this trains resilience. Marcus needs it to survive a hostile C-suite Q&A. Priya needs it to handle investor objections without deflecting. Devon needs it to build the automatic recovery response that makes oral argumentation possible. Without this feature, the app is just another recording tool.

**Where AI Enhances the Experience**
The AI reads the content of what the user just said — not just detects pauses — and generates contextually relevant follow-up questions. If the user says "our market is growing at 40% year-over-year," the AI does not ask a generic question; it asks "What's driving that growth rate and why would it continue?" This context-sensitivity is what makes the pressure feel real rather than scripted. The AI also adapts within a session — if the user handles the first three interruptions confidently, the AI escalates; if the user is clearly struggling, it doesn't pile on, but it does maintain its difficulty calibration.

**Fallback When AI Is Unavailable**
If cloud AI services are unavailable mid-session, the engine switches to a pre-generated local interruption library — a bank of scenario-appropriate interruptions and follow-up questions pre-cached on device. These are not context-sensitive (they don't reference what the user just said) but they are scenario-appropriate and difficulty-calibrated. Users are notified: "AI is responding from prepared questions — real-time context detection is temporarily unavailable." Sessions are not cancelled. Post-session coherence scoring (which requires AI analysis) is deferred until connectivity restores.

**Edge Cases**
- *User speaks in a language other than English*: Transcription continues but AI responses default to English. A note in session setup clarifies the app is optimized for English. Non-English transcription may reduce feedback accuracy; this is surfaced honestly in post-session feedback.
- *User is silent for an extended period*: After 10 seconds the AI offers a gentle nudge (Level 1–4) or a more direct prompt (Level 5–8). After 20 seconds a countdown to the next AI prompt appears. After 30 seconds the AI moves to its next intervention naturally.
- *User explicitly argues back against the AI*: The AI is programmed to maintain its challenge rather than capitulate — it is adversarial by design. It does not get "convinced" by the user's argument during a session, because the training value is in the user holding their ground, not in winning a debate.
- *Background noise corrupts transcription*: If audio quality drops below a quality threshold, a session notice appears: "Noisy environment detected — move to a quieter space for best results." Session continues regardless.
- *User speaks very quickly or very slowly*: Interruption timing is adaptive — it does not fire based on a fixed timer but on speech activity detection, so extremely fast or slow speakers still receive calibrated interventions.

### Feature 2: Honest AI Feedback with Transparent Scoring

**What It Does**
After every session, the app generates a detailed feedback report covering four scored dimensions: filler word rate (the frequency of "um," "uh," "like," "you know," "basically," and similar markers relative to total words spoken), pacing consistency (variance in words-per-minute across the session, with particular attention to spikes around interruption moments), response coherence (how directly and logically the user answered or responded to AI interventions — scored based on claim-evidence structure in their responses), and recovery speed (the time between an AI interruption and the user resuming coherent, forward-moving speech).

Each metric is scored on a 0–100 scale. The composite score is a weighted average of all four. The weighting is visible on screen. Every score is derived from the session transcript and audio analysis — not from AI opinion. The app shows users the evidence for their score: exact filler word count with timestamps, a pacing graph with marked intervals, specific moments flagged for coherence issues with the relevant audio clip accessible.

Scores are benchmarked against the user's own history — not against an abstract "good speaker" standard. A user who was at 42 last week and is now at 51 is improving, and that's what the dashboard communicates.

**Why It Matters**
The number one competitor complaint — across Orai, Vocal Image, and Credible — is that AI feedback feels arbitrary, inaccurate, or unexplainably positive. Sandra downloaded an app that told her she was "doing great" when she knew she wasn't. Marcus got told he said "um" 14 times with no context about when or why. Priya got a score with no explanation of how it was calculated. This feature addresses every one of those failure modes explicitly. Users will only trust the app if the feedback is honest, specific, and explainable.

**Where AI Enhances the Experience**
Filler word detection and pacing are computed deterministically from the transcript — rule-based, not AI-generated, so they cannot hallucinate. Coherence scoring uses AI analysis of the transcript to evaluate whether the user's responses addressed what was asked — but the AI is given the transcript and the AI's question, and asked to evaluate the relevance and structure of the response. This is a constrained, verifiable task, not open-ended opinion generation. The AI's coherence assessment is accompanied by a direct quote from the user's transcript that supports the assessment.

**Fallback When AI Is Unavailable**
Filler word rate, pacing, and recovery speed are all computable from transcript and audio data alone — these are delivered immediately. Coherence scoring requires AI analysis and is deferred. The feedback screen shows: "Coherence score pending — will appear when connectivity restores. All other metrics are accurate and available now."

**Edge Cases**
- *User speaks only one or two sentences total*: Feedback is still generated from available data, but a notice appears: "Short session — some metrics may be less accurate than in longer sessions."
- *Transcript quality was poor due to audio issues*: Any metric derived from a low-confidence transcript segment is flagged: "This segment had reduced audio quality — treat this score with caution."
- *User's score drops significantly from prior session*: No alarm, no guilt. A plain note: "Your score is lower than your last session. This is normal — particularly after increasing difficulty or running back-to-back sessions." Context is provided, not judgment.
- *User disputes a filler word count*: A "Review this" option on any metric allows users to flag it for review. On tap, the full transcript highlights every detected filler word instance. The user can see and judge each one. This doesn't change the score retroactively but builds trust in the system.
- *First session*: No trend lines, no benchmark comparisons. Metrics are presented as "This is your starting point." The framing is establishment of baseline, not evaluation.

## 6. Data & Content Requirements

### 6.1 Core Data Entities

**User Profile**
The app maintains a single profile per account capturing skill level, onboarding responses, preferred session length, and cumulative usage history. The profile is the anchor for all personalization — difficulty calibration, scenario unlocks, and progress benchmarking all derive from it. A user's profile is theirs; it should never be compared against other users or exposed in aggregate leaderboards.

**Session Record**
Every completed or abandoned pressure session produces a session record. This is the most important data entity in the app. It captures when the session happened, which scenario was used, how long it ran, the difficulty level at the time, and the full transcript of the exchange. Session records are the source of truth for all feedback and progress tracking. Partial sessions — where a user quit early — are still stored, because incomplete attempts are legitimate training data and useful for trend analysis.

**Feedback Report**
Each session record has exactly one associated feedback report, generated immediately after the session ends. The report contains scored dimensions (filler word rate, pacing, clarity, response coherence, confidence markers), plain-language explanations for each score, a comparison to the user's previous three sessions on the same scenario type, and specific moments from the transcript cited as evidence. Feedback reports are never retroactively modified once generated — historical accuracy matters more than corrections.

**Scenario**
A scenario is a structured speaking challenge with a defined context (e.g., "Series A investor pitch," "hostile panel Q&A," "impromptu speech on a random topic"), a difficulty band, a set of AI behavioral directives that govern how aggressively the pressure engine operates, and a tag indicating whether it is free-tier or premium. Scenarios are system-created content, not user-generated. Each scenario has a version number — when a scenario is updated, existing session records retain their link to the version they were played under, preserving historical comparability.

**Scenario Progress**
This entity tracks a user's relationship with each scenario: how many times they've attempted it, their highest score, their most recent score, and whether it is unlocked. It is what drives the progressive unlock system — scenarios at higher difficulty bands become available when performance thresholds are met on prerequisite scenarios.

**Subscription State**
A lightweight record of the user's current tier (free or premium), the subscription start date, the next renewal date, whether auto-renew is on, and pause status. This entity is the single source of truth for feature gating. It must be readable offline so the app never locks a user out of free-tier features due to a failed network call.

### 6.2 User-Generated vs. System-Generated Content

**User-generated:**
- Audio captured during sessions (stored locally by default, optionally synced)
- Session transcripts (derived from user speech)
- Implicitly: the behavioral pattern of a user's speech over time, which becomes the benchmark for their own progress scoring

**System-generated:**
- All scenario content, pressure tactics, and AI interviewer personas
- Feedback reports and all scores within them
- AI interruptions, follow-up questions, and topic pivots during sessions
- Monthly new scenarios added to the library
- Onboarding difficulty placement recommendation

There is no user-to-user content sharing, no social feed, and no community submissions in v1. This is deliberate. User-generated content moderation is a product surface the app is not ready to own yet, and the core value proposition requires no social layer to function.

---

### 6.3 Content the App Creates, Stores, and Displays

**In-session content:**
The app surfaces real-time visual cues during a session — a live transcript of what the user is saying, a subtle indicator when the AI is about to interrupt, and a running clock. The session view is intentionally minimal. Distraction during a pressure exercise undermines the training value.

**Post-session content:**
The feedback report is the primary post-session deliverable. It must be readable in under two minutes, scannable at a glance (scored dimensions displayed visually), and deep enough that a user who wants to understand exactly why they scored as they did can follow the evidence trail from score to transcript moment. The report is permanently stored and accessible from session history.

**Progress content:**
The progress view aggregates session records over time into trend visualizations — score trajectories per dimension, session frequency, and scenario completion rates. This view must clearly communicate whether the user is improving, plateauing, or regressing, without judgment. Plateaus are labeled as "consistent performance," not "no improvement."

**Scenario library content:**
Each scenario card displays its title, context description, difficulty band, expected duration, whether it is free or premium, and a short note on what pressure tactics the AI will use. Users are not surprised by what happens in a session — previewing the AI's behavior is part of setting appropriate expectations and reducing anxiety-driven drop-off.

---

### 6.4 Data Relationships That Matter

A user has many sessions. Each session belongs to exactly one scenario and one difficulty level snapshot. Each session produces one feedback report. A user's scenario progress is derived from their session history against that scenario — it is not stored redundantly but computed from session records. The subscription state determines which scenarios are accessible but never retroactively restricts access to session history or previously completed feedback reports. A user who downgrades from premium to free retains full read access to everything they earned while premium.

## 7. Non-Functional Requirements

### 7.1 Performance — As the User Experiences It

**Session startup:** A user who taps "Start Session" should hear the AI's opening prompt within three seconds on a standard LTE connection. Any longer and the psychological readiness built during the scenario setup screen dissipates. On poor network, the session should still start within five seconds using cached scenario assets, with a brief visual indicator that full features are active.

**Interruption latency:** The defining interaction in Cognitize is the AI interrupting the user mid-speech. For this to feel like real conversational pressure — rather than a glitchy chatbot — the interruption must fire within 800 milliseconds of the trigger condition being met. Latency above 1.5 seconds makes the interruption feel artificial and breaks immersion. This is the single most important performance requirement in the product.

**Feedback report generation:** After a session ends, the feedback report should appear within ten seconds. Users are in a heightened state immediately post-session and most receptive to feedback in that window. A spinner that runs for thirty seconds teaches users to ignore the feedback screen entirely.

**App launch to home screen:** Under four seconds on an iPhone 12 or newer, cold start. The app must not show a loading screen that makes users question whether it launched correctly.

**Scroll and animation:** All list scrolling and animated transitions must run at 60fps without jank. This is table stakes — a coaching app that feels sluggish signals low quality before the user has experienced a single session.

---

### 7.2 Reliability

The app must not crash during an active session under any circumstances that can be anticipated. A crash mid-session is the worst possible failure mode — it destroys the psychological state the user built up, and likely ends their engagement for the day. Session state must be checkpointed continuously so that if the app is interrupted by a phone call, backgrounded by the OS, or suffers an unexpected termination, the session can be recovered or at minimum the partial transcript and time-in-session are preserved.

Network failures during sessions must fail gracefully and silently. Users should not see API error messages, loading spinners, or blank states mid-session. The app degrades to a reduced-capability mode (local processing only) rather than stopping.

---

### 7.3 Accessibility

**Voice is the primary input modality** — which means the app must not require voice for any navigation, settings, or management action. Every non-session interaction in the app must be fully accessible via touch and compatible with VoiceOver (iOS). This is not optional accessibility polish; it is required because users with speech impediments or anxiety disorders are a meaningful portion of the target audience.

**Text legibility:** All feedback text, scenario descriptions, and score explanations must meet WCAG AA contrast ratios. Feedback reports contain nuanced language — small grey text on white backgrounds is actively hostile to the people who need to read it most carefully.

**Motion sensitivity:** The app uses animated pressure indicators during sessions. All non-essential animations must respect the iOS "Reduce Motion" accessibility setting. Users who are sensitive to motion effects should not be excluded from full functionality.

**Font scaling:** All text must respond correctly to iOS Dynamic Type settings up to the "Accessibility Extra Large" size without layout breakage. Users who need larger text to read comfortably should not find the feedback report unreadable.

**Color independence:** Score indicators and progress charts must not rely solely on color to convey meaning. Shape, label, and pattern must carry the information independently for users with color vision deficiencies.

---

### 7.4 Platform and Device Requirements

**iOS-first, iOS only for v1.** Target iOS 16 and above. The app must be tested on physical devices representing the installed base — iPhone 12 (A14 chip), iPhone 14, and iPhone 15 Pro as the high-end target. Simulator testing alone is insufficient for audio pipeline validation.

**No iPad-specific layout required for v1,** but the app must not be broken or unusable on iPad. It runs in iPhone compatibility mode on iPad — acceptable for v1, not a long-term position.

**Microphone access is required.** The app must handle microphone permission denial gracefully, with a clear, non-punishing explanation of why access is needed and a direct path to iOS Settings to grant it. A user who denies microphone access should still be able to explore the scenario library and review any existing session history.

**Background audio behavior:** When a session is running and the user backgrounds the app, the session pauses and notifies the user. The app does not attempt to continue recording in the background without explicit user awareness. This is both a privacy requirement and an App Store compliance requirement.

---

### 7.5 Offline Behavior

Cognitize's offline contract must be clearly defined and communicated to users — not discovered through frustration.

**Available offline:**
- Full access to session history and all feedback reports
- Scenario library browsing (with cached content)
- Progress and trend views
- Profile and settings management
- Any session that uses the on-device processing path for speech recognition

**Requires connectivity:**
- Starting a new session in full AI mode (requires cloud speech-to-text and GPT-4o)
- Subscription management
- Syncing session data to cloud backup

When the user attempts to start a session offline, the app must clearly communicate what will and won't work — not silently fail or show a generic error. If a degraded offline session mode is available, offer it explicitly with a plain-language description of what is different. Never show a spinning loader to a user who has no network — detect offline state proactively and present a useful state instead.

## 8. Success Criteria & Scope

### 8.1 North Star Metric

**Weekly Active Pressured Users (WAPU):** the number of distinct users who complete at least one full pressure simulation session in a given week.

This is the north star because it captures the core behavior that creates value — not downloads, not sign-ups, not logins, but completed sessions. A user who downloads the app and never runs a session has received no value. A user who runs sessions weekly is building a real practice. Everything the product does should ultimately move WAPU upward.

---

### 8.2 Leading Indicators of Success

These are the metrics that, if trending correctly, predict WAPU growth before it shows up in the aggregate number.

**Session completion rate:** The percentage of started sessions that run to completion rather than being abandoned. Target: 70% or above at 30 days post-launch. A rate below 50% signals that the pressure level, session length, or in-session experience is wrong — users are opting out before getting value.

**Day-7 retention:** The percentage of new users who return and complete a session within their first seven days. Target: 30% or above. Below 20% is a sign that the onboarding experience or the first session fails to create the "I need to come back" reaction.

**Feedback report view rate:** The percentage of completed sessions where the user opens and spends more than 20 seconds on the feedback report. Target: 65% or above. Users who don't read their feedback are not building the self-awareness loop that creates retention.

**Free-to-premium conversion rate:** Among users who have completed five or more sessions on the free tier, the percentage who upgrade. Target: 8–12% at 60 days. Conversion below 5% after meaningful usage suggests the premium value proposition is unclear or the free tier is too complete; above 15% early may indicate the free tier is too restrictive.

**Subscription cancellation rate:** Monthly subscriber churn target below 6%. Paired with an exit survey, churn above this threshold triggers investigation — not retention dark patterns.

---

### 8.3 AI Quality Bars

These are minimum acceptable performance thresholds. Below these, the product should not ship.

**Interruption timing accuracy:** At least 90% of AI interruptions must fire at a grammatically plausible breakpoint — mid-sentence but not mid-word, and not during a silent pause the user is using to think. Interruptions that feel random or robotically timed destroy the pressure-simulation illusion.

**Filler word detection accuracy:** The filler word counter in feedback reports must achieve at least 85% precision and 80% recall against a human-annotated ground truth set of 20 diverse session transcripts. A feedback report that tells a user they said "um" 12 times when they said it 4 times destroys trust in all scores, not just that one.

**Feedback coherence:** Every post-session feedback report must cite at least two specific transcript moments as evidence for the scores given. Generic feedback ("you could improve your pacing") with no connection to what the user actually said is not acceptable. 100% of generated reports must pass this check before being shown.

**Interruption relevance:** At least 80% of AI follow-up questions must be topically relevant to what the user was saying at the time of interruption, as judged by a human reviewer sampling 50 sessions. Follow-up questions that ignore context and feel scripted defeat the entire product premise.

**No hallucinated scores:** Filler word counts, pacing metrics, and session duration figures in feedback reports must be deterministically computed — not AI-generated estimates — and must be 100% accurate against the transcript. There is zero tolerance for fabricated quantitative claims in feedback.

### 8.5 Open Questions for Development

1. **Interruption opt-out during session:** Should users be able to temporarily pause AI interruptions mid-session (e.g., tap a "no interruptions" toggle) or does enabling this undermine the core training premise? There is a real tension between user control and training integrity.

2. **Session length defaults:** The brief specifies five-minute sessions as a baseline. Should the app enforce a minimum session length before feedback is generated, or should a two-minute session produce a (clearly labeled, partial) report? Abandoned short sessions may still contain useful feedback.

3. **Feedback delivery timing:** Immediate post-session feedback is the current plan. Research on deliberate practice suggests a short reflection pause before feedback improves retention. Is it worth testing a 30-second "what do you think went well?" prompt before showing the report?

4. **Difficulty auto-adjustment:** Should the pressure engine auto-adjust difficulty within a session based on real-time performance signals (e.g., becoming less aggressive if the user shows signs of distress), or should difficulty be fixed at session start and only changeable between sessions? Auto-adjustment is more adaptive but harder to make feel natural.

5. **Audio storage consent in EU:** Users in GDPR jurisdictions must explicitly consent to cloud audio storage. The default is local-only. Should EU users be presented with a clearly different onboarding storage flow, or is a universal privacy-first default (local-only for everyone) simpler and more defensible?

6. **Premium trial length:** Three days versus seven days for the premium trial. Three days creates urgency but may not be enough time for a busy professional to run five meaningful sessions and feel the premium value. Seven days costs more per conversion in API spend. What is the right trade-off?

## 9. Anti-Patterns

### 9.1 Things That Must Not Be Built

**Paywalling the core experience.**
Orai, Speeko, and Credible's most-cited failure is that the meaningful functionality starts behind a subscription screen. Cognitize's free tier must include real pressure simulation, real feedback, and real progress tracking — not a watered-down demo. If a free user cannot genuinely evaluate whether the app improves their speaking, the pricing model has failed. Locking feedback reports, limiting sessions to under two minutes for free users, or disabling post-session scores on the free tier are all explicitly forbidden.

**Black-box AI scoring.**
Vocal Image and Credible both receive user complaints about scores that feel arbitrary or inconsistent with perceived performance. Any score shown in Cognitize must have a visible explanation of how it was calculated, and quantitative metrics (filler word counts, session duration, pacing rate) must be deterministically computed — never estimated by a language model. "Confidence: 6.2/10" with no explanation is not a feedback report. It is a random number generator with a decimal point.

**Dark patterns in subscription management.**
No hidden trial sign-ups. No pre-checked auto-renewal boxes. No cancellation flows that require navigating to a website. No "pause" option that is harder to find than the cancel button (pause should be equally or more prominent — it serves the user). No price changes without clear advance notification. No charges without renewal reminders. Any of these patterns, individually, will generate App Store reviews that poison the product's reputation and trigger platform scrutiny.

**Aggressive re-engagement notifications.**
Push notifications that say "You haven't practiced in 3 days — your skills are getting rusty!" are manipulative. Cognitize's notifications must be informational and user-controlled. Acceptable: "Your weekly session reminder — you've set aside Tuesday evenings." Unacceptable: guilt, urgency manufacturing, or false claims about skill decay. Users who opt out of notifications must never be prompted again to re-enable them.

**Streaks and gamification as retention mechanics.**
Streak counters that reset to zero when a user misses a day — and prominently display that reset — are anxiety-inducing mechanics that serve the app's retention metrics at the expense of the user's relationship with practice. Cognitize does not use streaks. Progress is cumulative and forward-looking. Missing a week of practice is represented as a gap in a chart, not a punitive reset of an achievement.

**Leaderboards or social comparison.**
Public speaking anxiety is the explicit problem Cognitize addresses. Showing users how they rank against other users — or how their "confidence score" compares to the top percentile — is directly counter to the product's purpose. Progress is always benchmarked against the user's own history, never against the user base.

**Generic AI feedback disconnected from actual user speech.**
Feedback that could have been written before the session happened — "try to speak more slowly and avoid filler words" — is not coaching. It is content. Every piece of feedback in a report must reference something the user actually said in that session. Generic advice is not feedback; it is filler content that trains users to stop reading their reports.

---

### 9.2 Pitfalls to Avoid

**Optimizing for session starts instead of session completions.**
It is tempting to push users into sessions frequently — reminders, prompts, "quick 2-minute drill" CTAs. But the metric that matters is completed sessions with read feedback reports. A user who starts eight sessions and abandons them all is not practicing; they are guilt-managing. Design the session setup and scenario selection to set appropriate expectations so users only start sessions they intend to finish.

**Making difficulty the user's problem.**
If a beginner is overwhelmed by the Intense difficulty setting, that is a product failure — not a user failure. Difficulty calibration must be conservative by default. Onboarding placement should err on the side of starting users lower than they think they should be. The damage from a user quitting because they were frustrated far exceeds the damage from a user thinking their first session was too easy.

**Treating audio latency as a technical footnote.**
The interruption mechanic is the product. If the audio pipeline introduces even 400 milliseconds of unexpected latency, the experience degrades from "real conversational pressure" to "talking to a lag-prone chatbot." Audio performance must be treated as a first-class product requirement with defined quality gates — not an infrastructure concern addressed after feature development.

**Over-explaining the AI in the UI.**
Users do not need to know which speech recognition model is running or what prompt structure governs the pressure engine. In-app explanations of the AI that go beyond "here is how your score was calculated" introduce complexity that does not help users improve. Technical transparency lives in the privacy policy and on a dedicated methodology page. It does not live in the post-session feedback view.

**Launching with too many scenarios.**
Shipping thirty scenarios on launch day sounds like high value. In practice, it diffuses focus, introduces more surface area for bugs, and means none of the scenarios have been refined through real user feedback. Launch with six well-tested, genuinely pressure-calibrated scenarios that work excellently. Add a new scenario monthly. A library that grows in response to real usage is more trustworthy than a library that was padded at launch to look impressive.

**Conflating "more feedback" with "better feedback."**
Longer feedback reports are not more useful than shorter ones. A feedback report that surfaces three specific, actionable observations with transcript evidence is more valuable than a report with twelve observations that dilute each other. Edit feedback ruthlessly. The most important output of any session should be one thing the user can try differently next time — surfaced prominently, not buried in a wall of analysis.
